# Branded athletic footwear & apparel — evaluation framework

> Personal playbook for evaluating businesses whose economics depend on owning a sports/athletic brand and selling shoes, apparel, and accessories under it. Covers global incumbents (Nike, Adidas, Puma), insurgents (On, Hoka/Deckers, Lululemon, Asics), and specialists (Skechers, Under Armour, Brooks).
> Append-only. Refine every time you study a new name in this space.

**Last updated:** 2026-05-20

---

## What this industry is

Branded athletic footwear & apparel is the business of charging a 5-10x markup on a $20-30 manufactured pair of shoes (or piece of technical apparel) because the consumer believes the swoosh / three stripes / cloud / rocker plate signals identity, performance, or status. Demand is split between **performance use** (running, basketball, training, soccer, outdoor) — where product credibility on the foot of elite athletes matters — and **lifestyle/sportswear** — where the brand operates as fashion, cycling in and out of cultural relevance on a 3-7 year basis. Almost all manufacturing is outsourced to Vietnam, Indonesia, China, and increasingly India/Cambodia; the brand owner's job is design, athlete/team endorsement, marketing, distribution mix, and inventory discipline. The economics work when the brand commands gross margin in the high-40s to low-50s and operating margin in the low-to-mid teens through cycles, with capital-light returns on tangible capital well above WACC. The economics fail catastrophically when the brand loses cultural permission and the company is left with the cost structure of a premium brand but the pricing power of a commodity supplier — at which point inventory writedowns, gross margin compression, and SG&A deleverage all hit simultaneously.

## Industry structure

- **Concentration:** global oligopoly at the top (Nike + Adidas together ~30-35% of the global athletic footwear market) with a long tail of category specialists. Running is more fragmented (Brooks, Hoka, Asics, New Balance, On all credible); basketball is a Nike near-monopoly; soccer is Nike/Adidas/Puma; women's training/yoga is a Lululemon-led category.
- **Customer:** B2C ultimately, but the **immediate** customer is split between wholesale (Foot Locker, Dick's, JD Sports, department stores, run specialty) and direct (own retail stores, .com, apps). DTC mix has been the dominant strategic variable of the last decade — Nike's overshoot of DTC at the expense of wholesale (2017-2024) is the cautionary tale every operator in the industry now references.
- **Cyclicality:** moderately cyclical with secular tailwinds. Athleisure/casualisation of dress codes is a 20-year unfinished trend. Recession sensitivity is real but muted vs apparel generally — performance categories are stickier than fashion. The bigger cycle is **brand-heat cycles**: brands rise and fall on 5-10 year cultural cycles independent of GDP.
- **Capital intensity:** asset-light by design. PP&E is mostly retail stores and a small amount of innovation/design infrastructure. PP&E / revenue typically 5-10%. Inventory and receivables are the real working-capital game.
- **Operating leverage:** moderate to high. Fixed brand spend, designers, athlete contracts, and lease obligations don't flex down quickly when revenue rolls over. The fast-incumbent (Nike, Adidas) operating margins have a -200 to +200 bps annual range depending on the cycle.
- **Regulatory regime:** light-touch in the main markets, but exposed to **tariffs** (this is now first-order — US tariffs on China/Vietnam/Indonesia/Cambodia footwear are a major 2025-2026 swing factor), labour-standards scrutiny, IP/counterfeit enforcement, advertising regulation around minors and athlete endorsements.
- **Geographic concentration:** revenue split is typically ~40% North America, ~25-30% EMEA, ~15-20% Greater China, ~10-15% APAC/LatAm. Greater China is the strategic swing factor for the global names — when it works it's the highest-margin region; when it doesn't, the consensus growth narrative dies.

## What makes a great business in this industry

The 6-7 traits that separate compounders from also-rans:

1. **Cultural permission to charge.** This is the heart of the franchise. It's the answer to "why does a kid pay $180 for a shoe whose all-in cost is $25?" Cultural permission is built over decades through athlete endorsements that worked, marketing campaigns that landed, products that broke new ground, and proximity to the cultural moment (hip-hop and Jordan for Nike in the 80s-90s; Stan Smith/Yeezy for Adidas in the 2010s; Salomon for late-2020s gorpcore; Hoka for the mid-2020s running boom). **The hardest thing to assess and the most important.** A brand that has lost cultural permission can sustain reported financials for 2-3 years on inertia, then crater.
2. **Innovation pipeline that compounds at the elite level and trickles down.** Vaporfly/Alphafly for Nike, Boost for Adidas, the rocker geometry for Hoka, the CloudTec system for On — performance product breakthroughs that win on the foot of pros translate into lifestyle/recreational demand for 5-10 years. A company without a credible R&D engine ends up being a fashion-cycle bet.
3. **Disciplined wholesale-DTC balance.** Pure DTC is a trap — it strands shelf space, narrows distribution, and inverts retailer incentives to push competitors. Pure wholesale is a trap on the other side — margin leaks to retailers, no direct customer data, vulnerability to retailer consolidation. The right mix is 60-65% wholesale / 35-40% DTC with curated wholesale partnerships and DTC focused on premium/full-price product. Nike learned this the hard way 2017-2024.
4. **Inventory discipline.** The single most reliable operational tell. Brands that consistently grow inventory in line with or below revenue tend to have pricing power and product-market fit. Brands that consistently grow inventory ahead of revenue are about to take writedowns. Watch days inventory outstanding (DIO) trend over 8 quarters and gross margin sequential moves — those two together flag trouble 2-3 quarters before earnings.
5. **Operating margin defended through cycles.** Industry-leading operators sustain 12-15% operating margin through cycles. Margins below 8% sustained for more than a year usually signal brand erosion, not just a tough year. The mid-teens operating margin is the rough test of "still has cultural permission to charge."
6. **Capital-light return on tangible capital.** ROIC ex-goodwill north of 25% should be table stakes for a healthy branded business. Below that — usually means the brand has lost the right to its pricing power or has accumulated inventory/retail asset bloat.
7. **Founder/long-tenure operator culture.** This industry rewards operators who think in decades. Phil Knight (Nike's first 40 years), Kasper Rorsted/Bjorn Gulden (Adidas pivot), Chip Wilson (Lululemon founding), Mark Parker (Nike 2006-2020 in the strong years) — long-tenure operators with brand instincts have outperformed professionalised CEO churn. Sustained activist-driven or PE-driven turnover at the top is usually a red flag, even if the short-term financials look reasonable.

## Key drivers of returns

What makes the **stock** work over a 5-year holding — not what makes the next quarter:

- **Where the brand sits in the cultural cycle when you buy.** Buying a brand mid-rise (cultural heat building, sell-through accelerating, wholesale demanding more allocation) has been the highest-return entry point historically. Buying mid-decline (gross margin contracting, sell-through slowing, wholesale partners adding shelf space to competitors) has been catastrophic — looks cheap on TTM, gets cheaper.
- **Operating margin trajectory, not absolute level.** A 13% margin going to 15% earns the stock; a 15% margin going to 13% loses it. Same logic on gross margin.
- **Inventory-to-sales ratio over a multi-quarter window.** This signals brand health 2-3 quarters before it shows up in revenue.
- **Wholesale partner reordering and sell-through, not sell-in.** Sell-in (Nike's reported wholesale revenue) can be financed; sell-through (what consumers actually buy off shelves at Foot Locker / Dick's) is the truth.
- **Greater China direction.** For the global incumbents, China is the single biggest swing variable on TSR over a 5-year window. Local brands (Anta, Li-Ning) winning is the structural risk that didn't exist 10 years ago.
- **Capital allocation through the cycle.** Buybacks executed at high multiples (Nike 2021) destroy value; buybacks at trough multiples create it. M&A in this industry is almost always destructive — be skeptical of any deal beyond bolt-ons.
- **Multiple compression/expansion as a function of perceived terminal brand value.** This is the value vs growth axis applied to brands. If the market shifts from "Nike is a forever franchise" to "Nike is structurally challenged," the multiple goes from 30x to 15x and dominates fundamentals.

## Industry-specific KPIs

Generic metrics are mostly fine here (this is not energy or finance), but the following are the operational tells that matter most:

| Metric | What good looks like | What concerning looks like | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gross margin | 45-50%+ sustained; sequential trajectory positive | 5%+ erosion in 2 quarters; >300 bps below 5-year average | The brand's pricing power expressed as a number. Sustained GM erosion = brand erosion. |
| Operating margin | 12-15% through-cycle | Sub-8% sustained or compressing 200+ bps/yr | The mid-teens test for whether the brand still earns the right to charge. |
| Inventory days (DIO) | Flat or declining as revenue grows | Inventory growth > revenue growth for 2+ quarters | Single best forward indicator of upcoming markdowns and GM compression. |
| Sell-through at top-3 wholesale partners | Outperforming the category | Underperforming the category at Foot Locker/Dick's | What consumers actually buy. Sell-in lies; sell-through doesn't. |
| Full-price mix / promotion days | High and stable | Rising promo days, growing markdown allowances to wholesale | The first place brand erosion shows up — discounting to clear inventory. |
| Greater China revenue growth (global incumbents) | +mid-single digits through cycles | Sustained negative growth, especially with online declines outpacing physical | China rolling over has cost more in the global names' multiples than any other single factor 2022-2026. |
| Athlete/team endorsement renewal economics | Long-term deals at flat or modestly rising rates | Losing key athletes to competitors; ballooning endorsement spend without revenue follow-through | Cultural permission expressed contractually. |
| % revenue from products <2 years old (innovation pipeline) | 25%+ | <15% sustained | The innovation engine is working or it's not. |
| DTC vs wholesale mix and trajectory | 35-45% DTC, stable; wholesale partnerships strong | Either extreme; rapid mix swings | The Nike DTC-overshoot lesson. |
| ROIC ex-goodwill | 25%+ | Sub-15% | The capital-light brand model is intact or it's not. |

## Valuation framework

This is a P/E and FCF yield industry. EV/EBITDA is fine as a cross-check but isn't the primary lens — capital intensity is low enough that EBITDA and FCF don't diverge meaningfully. Revenue multiples (EV/Sales) are noise for incumbents but the only available metric for pre-profit insurgents (On in its early years).

- **Primary multiple:** forward P/E (12-18m out) on the global incumbents. FCF yield as the income-style cross-check.
- **Secondary cross-checks:** EV/EBITDA mid-cycle, EV/Sales vs historical band, P/E vs same-region peers, dividend yield vs treasuries (only meaningful at deep cyclical lows for the mature names).
- **Historical range:** Nike has traded 18-35x forward P/E over 15 years; <20x is rare and has historically marked bottoms. Adidas has traded 15-40x with more volatility. Insurgents like On / Deckers have ranged 25-60x depending on growth phase.
- **What drives the multiple up vs down:** brand-heat narrative, Greater China outlook, gross margin trajectory, comparison vs prior cycle peak, sentiment on whether DTC/wholesale rebalance is "complete."
- **When does the multiple lie?** Two situations to be careful of: (a) **Trough multiples on peak earnings** — branded companies look cheap on the year they last earned through-cycle margins; the bear case is that those margins were the peak. (b) **Premium multiples on growth that's actually fashion-cycle pull-forward** — applies to insurgent brands during their hype phase (e.g. Lululemon in 2020-2021, On in 2024).

## Red flags specific to this industry

Things that look fine in generic analysis but signal real problems here:

- **Inventory growing materially faster than revenue for 2+ quarters.** The single most reliable forward signal of GM and operating margin compression. Especially serious if combined with rising promotional intensity.
- **Falling sell-through at top wholesale partners while reported wholesale revenue holds up.** Means the brand is being financed onto shelves, not pulled off them. Reported revenue lags consumer reality by 1-3 quarters.
- **A spate of senior product/marketing departures.** Branded businesses live or die on the product and brand teams. CFO turnover is noise; CMO and Chief Product Officer turnover at the same time is a real signal.
- **"DTC pivot" or "consumer direct" rhetoric not accompanied by maintained wholesale relationships.** This was the Nike 2017-2024 mistake. When a brand voluntarily exits Amazon, exits Foot Locker shelves, or cuts off run specialty stores in favor of own retail, watch closely.
- **Athlete endorsement losses at the top of the roster.** When a brand loses LeBron-class athletes to a competitor, or fails to sign the next generation, the cultural pipeline is already broken even if revenue hasn't reflected it.
- **Greater China online sales declining materially faster than physical retail.** Online is leading indicator; physical lags by a year. Online -20% with physical flat means physical is going to -10% next year.
- **Aggressive promotional calendars on full-price product (not just clearance).** Discounting hero franchises (Air Max, Jordan, Stan Smith) — vs just clearing seasonal stock — means the brand is buying revenue and will pay back in margin.
- **Sustained share buybacks at peak multiples.** Companies that bought back stock at 30-35x P/E in 2021 paid for it later. The IR deck calls these "returning capital"; the cycle calls them "destroying capital."

## Common bull-case archetypes

The stories that typically lead investors to overpay in this industry:

- **"The brand is forever — buy on dips."** True over very long horizons for the top 2-3 brands; false over 5-year horizons when the brand is mid-decline. The Nike 2024-2026 drawdown is the most recent reminder.
- **"DTC margin expansion."** The story that drove Nike's 2017-2021 multiple expansion. Real for a while; reversed brutally when the structural costs of running retail and .com became visible and wholesale partners pulled shelf space.
- **"China is a $XXB opportunity."** The same chart was drawn every year from 2010 to 2023. The structural shift to local brands (Anta, Li-Ning, Bosideng) means this opportunity may be permanently impaired for foreign incumbents — at least at the same margins.
- **"Insurgent X is the next Nike."** Survivors of this archetype are rare. On Holding and Hoka are credible; the graveyard (Reebok at peak, Fila revivals, Under Armour 2014-2017) is full.

## Common bear-case archetypes

The stories that typically signal genuine trouble vs noise:

- **"The brand has lost the kids."** When middle/high-school cohort surveys show the brand losing share to insurgents — and this persists for 4+ quarters — the next 5 years are at risk. This is what got Under Armour 2017+ and is the threat to Nike in run-specialty 2024-2026.
- **"Wholesale won't take more product."** When Foot Locker or Dick's stops asking for allocation and starts asking for markdown allowances, the brand is in trouble whether or not the consumer narrative says so.
- **"Inventory is bloated."** Persistent inventory overhang means quarters of GM pressure ahead even after the consumer comps stabilise.
- **"The CEO is fighting the last war."** Operators who built their resumes in one cycle (DTC, China growth, athleisure) often double down on those theses when the cycle turns.

## Capital allocation patterns

How good operators in this industry deploy cash:

- **Buybacks at trough valuations are the highest-return use of capital.** Nike 2008-2012, Adidas 2009-2012, even Lululemon during the 2014-2015 drawdown. The companies that lever up to buy back near peak multiples (Nike 2021) destroy as much value as they created the prior decade.
- **Dividends are appropriate for the mature global incumbents.** Nike, Adidas, Puma. Insurgents (Hoka via Deckers, On, Lululemon historically) reinvest instead.
- **M&A is almost universally destructive.** Nike acquired Umbro (sold at loss), Cole Haan (sold at loss), Hurley (sold at loss). Adidas acquired Reebok (sold at loss). The rare exception is bolt-on brand acquisitions that genuinely diversify (Deckers buying Hoka in 2012 is the canonical good one). **Default to skepticism on any deal beyond a small bolt-on.**
- **Capex is structurally light** — retail expansion, technology, occasional manufacturing investment. Read the capex breakdown: too much retail capex = DTC overshoot brewing.

## Mental-model crossover risks

Assumptions from other frameworks that **do not transfer** here:

- **"Scale wins."** Only partially. Scale matters for athlete endorsement budgets, manufacturing terms, and supply chain. But scale alone does NOT defend the brand against insurgents. Nike has 10x On's revenue and is losing run-specialty share. Brand-heat does not respect scale.
- **"Recurring revenue is stable."** There's no recurring revenue here. Every shoe is a one-time discretionary purchase. The closest analogue to recurrence is brand loyalty/repeat purchase rate, but that decays much faster than SaaS retention.
- **"Network effects compound."** No real network effects in apparel/footwear. The closest is the gravitational effect of being where the cultural moment is (LeBron wearing your shoes, Bad Bunny wearing your fits), but this can reverse in a single celebrity cycle.
- **"Sticky enterprise customer."** Wholesale partners (Foot Locker, Dick's, JD Sports) are NOT sticky in the SaaS sense. They allocate shelf space quarterly and will shift allocations to whoever has the heat. Don't model wholesale revenue as recurring.
- **"Founder/promoter-led businesses are special" applies, but with a twist.** Long-tenure operators (Knight, Gulden, Wilson at his peak) outperform, but unlike software, branded businesses can be run successfully by long-tenured operators who didn't found the company — Mark Parker at Nike 2006-2020 is an example. The signal is tenure + cultural fluency, not founder status per se.
- **"Cyclically cheap = buying opportunity."** Branded businesses look cheap on the way down for years before they bottom. Multiple compression from 30x to 12x can take 3-5 years. Trough multiples on peak margins (i.e. peak margins were the cyclical/cultural peak) is the most expensive trap in this industry.

## What I've learned over time

Append-only log. Every time you do a deep dive or close a position, write back what you learned.

- 2026-05-20 (NKE deep-dive, source: framework-bootstrap + initial dive): The Nike DTC overshoot 2017-2024 is the case study every other operator references. The full cycle ran ~7 years from "brilliant strategic move" (2017) to "obvious mistake" (2024) — the consensus took most of those 7 years to break. Lesson: when a branded business pivots distribution strategy, the verdict on the pivot is rarely available inside a 2-3 year window — and the multiple expands or compresses on consensus rather than reality. Bring extra skepticism to "DTC is the future" or "wholesale-first is the future" framings while the cycle is still unfolding.
