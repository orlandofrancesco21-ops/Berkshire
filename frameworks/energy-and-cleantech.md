# Energy & Cleantech — evaluation framework

> Personal playbook for evaluating businesses across the energy umbrella: oil & gas (upstream, midstream, downstream, services), power generation (regulated, merchant, renewable), and the cleantech adjacencies (solar/wind manufacturing, batteries, hydrogen, EVs, grid tech, nuclear/uranium).

## What this industry is

Energy is the business of converting physical resources or engineered systems into usable power, fuel, or heat — and selling the result as a commodity (mostly), a regulated service, or a contracted offtake. Demand at the end of the chain is one of the most stable in the global economy (everyone needs electricity, transport fuel, and heat); the **margin** at any given point in the chain is anything but stable, because each link is a different type of business: extraction (cost-curve game), transport/storage (utility-like), refining/conversion (spread game), distribution (regulated monopoly), and now technology-led generation (manufacturing/scale game). Cleantech is layered on top — wind, solar, batteries, hydrogen, EVs, grid software — and depending on the slice, behaves like industrial manufacturing, project finance, or a regulated utility add-on. The "energy transition" is the cross-cutting force reshaping all of it: legacy hydrocarbons face stranded-asset risk on a 30-year horizon; cleantech faces commoditization, subsidy dependency, and capital-cycle risk now.

## Industry structure (umbrella view)

- **Concentration:** mixed — oligopolies (offshore drillers post-2020, large integrated majors, regional pipeline networks, regulated utilities by territory, big-3 services); fragmented (US E&P, refining, solar manufacturing, EV charging)
- **Customer:** mostly B2B (refiners → wholesalers, services → E&P, midstream → producers, OEMs → utilities). B2C at the meter (retail power/gas) and at the pump. Increasingly B2G via subsidy programs (IRA, EU Green Deal, REPowerEU)
- **Cyclicality:** **bimodal.** Commodity-exposed businesses (E&P, services, refining, merchant power, manufacturing) are among the most cyclical in the entire equity market. Regulated/contracted businesses (utilities, midstream, YieldCos) are bond-like.
- **Capital intensity:** among the highest in any sector. PP&E to revenue >1x is common; for utilities and pipelines often 4-6x.
- **Operating leverage:** extreme for commodity businesses (E&P, refining, services); structurally low for regulated.
- **Regulatory regime:** heavy and constantly shifting. IRA (US), EU ETS, methane rules, state PUCs, FERC, permitting reform debates, transition mandates, carbon pricing. Regulation **is** the industry in places — utility rate base, renewable PPA economics, LNG export licensing.
- **Geographic concentration:** oil is global, gas is regional (basis differentials matter — Henry Hub vs JKM vs TTF), power is national/state-level. Cleantech manufacturing has a strong China center of gravity; the policy moat (or lack of it) for Western producers is the key variable.

## What makes a great business in this industry

The 5-7 traits that distinguish long-term compounders from also-rans across the umbrella:

1. **Low position on the global cost curve.** Across virtually every commodity-exposed sub-sector, the businesses that compound through cycles are those that can be profitable at the price level that bankrupts the marginal producer. For E&P this is breakeven WTI / Henry Hub; for refining it's cash operating cost per barrel; for renewables manufacturing it's $/W landed cost; for nuclear/uranium miners it's AISC. **If you can't articulate where the company sits on the global cost curve in two minutes, you don't yet understand the business.**
2. **Long-life, hard-to-replicate assets.** Long inventory of drilling locations, long-lived contracted pipelines, regulated rate base in a constructive jurisdiction, decades-long PPAs, world-class oil sands resources, irreplaceable LNG export terminals, premier interconnection queue positions. The opposite — assets with short half-lives or commoditized economics — almost always destroy capital over a full cycle.
3. **Counter-cyclical capital allocation.** The single most reliable predictor of long-term TSR in commodity energy is whether management invested when assets were cheap and returned cash when they were expensive. Almost everyone says they do this; almost no one actually does. Audit it through the cycle data — capex levels, M&A timing, buyback timing — not through the IR deck.
4. **Contracted or regulated cash-flow base.** Pipelines on take-or-pay contracts, utilities with constructive regulatory frameworks, YieldCos with long-dated PPAs, LNG facilities on tolling agreements. Even within commodity-exposed businesses, the proportion of contracted/hedged cash flow is what determines whether they can survive a 24-month price collapse without dilutive capital raises.
5. **Operating culture of cost discipline.** Per-unit cost trends — F&D for E&P, OPEX/MWh for IPPs, $/W for solar makers, cost per kg for hydrogen players, cost-per-ton for miners — that decline through cycles regardless of commodity prices. This is more durable than commodity moves and more predictive of long-term returns than almost any other operational metric.
6. **Optionality without overpaying for it.** Inventory depth (E&P), brownfield expansion (midstream/utilities), interconnect queue position (renewables), permitted capacity beyond contracted (LNG), ability to bolt on capacity at lower-than-greenfield cost. The best operators have this baked in cheaply; the worst ones buy it at the top of the cycle through expensive M&A.
7. **Balance sheet that lets you stay seated through the trough.** Net debt/EBITDA at **mid-cycle** is the right test, not at peak. The graveyard of energy investing is full of companies that looked cheap on peak earnings, levered into the cycle, and were forced to issue equity at the bottom (CHK, MUR pre-recovery, multiple offshore drillers in 2015-2020, several solar names 2011-2013).

## Key drivers of returns

What makes the **stock** work over a 5-year holding period — not what makes earnings beat next quarter:

- **Where you bought relative to mid-cycle commodity prices.** For commodity-exposed names, this dominates everything else. Buying at strip prices well above long-term marginal cost has lost more money in energy than any other single factor.
- **Capital allocation through one full cycle.** Not the IR-deck version — the actual track record of buybacks/dividends per share vs share-count growth and capex through the down-cycle.
- **Cost-curve position improvement** (or deterioration). The best E&Ps have moved down the cost curve over a decade through inventory upgrades and operating efficiency; mediocre ones bounce around without structural improvement.
- **Regulatory outcomes** for utilities, renewables, and midstream — rate cases, IRA changes, permitting reform, FERC shifts, state-level RPS mandates, FID approvals.
- **Multiple re-rate as a function of perceived terminal value.** Energy traded at deep discounts 2015-2020 partly on transition fears; some of that mean-reverted post-2022. Where you sit on the "is this a melting ice cube?" debate dominates the multiple as much as fundamentals.
- **Demand mix shifts:** EV adoption rate (oil demand), data-center load growth (utilities, gas, nuclear), industrial electrification, AI power consumption (the dominant 2024-2026 narrative), heat-pump adoption, China demand growth/decoupling.
- **The capital cycle.** Capacity gets added when prices are high; takes 3-7 years to come online; arrives just as the cycle has rolled. The classic Marathon-style capital-cycle framework is more applicable to energy than almost any other sector.

## Industry-specific KPIs

Generic metrics (P/E, gross margin) are **especially noise-prone here**. Use the right per-sub-sector KPIs from the navigator. The umbrella metrics that matter across the board:

| Metric | What good looks like | What concerning looks like | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cost-curve position (sub-sector specific) | Bottom or 2nd quartile globally | 3rd or 4th quartile | Determines who survives troughs and earns the duration of the up-cycle |
| Cash returns per share / total payout yield | 6%+ sustainable through cycles, growing | Distributions funded by issuance or debt | Energy compounding stories are usually **return-of-capital stories**, not growth stories |
| Reinvestment rate (capex / CFO) | <60% for mature E&P; <50% for refiners ex-turnarounds; contractually-driven for midstream/utilities | 100%+ for non-growth names | Distinguishes harvesters from capital-burners |
| Net debt / EBITDA at **mid-cycle** prices | <1.5x for cyclicals; <5x for utilities (matched to long-life assets); <4.5x for midstream | Above these at mid-cycle, and meaningfully higher at trough | The right balance-sheet test is mid-cycle, not TTM; many names look fine on TTM and are deeply leveraged at trough |
| ROACE / ROCE through-the-cycle average | Consistently above cost of capital across a full cycle (>10-12% nominal for cyclicals; ≥ allowed ROE for utilities) | Below WACC across a full cycle = capital destruction regardless of headline EPS | Most reliable cross-sub-sector profitability test |
| **Reserve life / inventory years** (E&P, miners) | 10+ years of high-quality inventory at flat prices | <5 years; growth dependent on M&A or step-out exploration | Tells you whether the business has runway or is harvesting toward extinction |
| **DCF coverage ratio** (midstream/MLPs) | >1.4x distribution coverage | <1.1x, especially with growth capex spend | Coverage <1x has historically preceded distribution cuts and equity blow-ups |
| **Allowed ROE vs achieved ROE** (utilities) | Achieved within 50bps of allowed | Persistent shortfall (regulatory lag, opex overruns, disallowances) | Tells you about regulatory constructiveness AND operational quality |
| **Capacity factor & PPA price** (renewables) | Capacity factor in line with resource quality (35-45% good wind; 25-30% utility solar); PPA prices stable/escalating | Below resource norms; PPA repricing risk on rolloff | The actual project economics, not the brochure |
| **Bookings / book-to-bill** (services, equipment) | Sustained >1.0x with rising backlog at improving pricing | Backlog growing but at flat/declining margins (volume-for-volume tradeoff) | Where the cycle is heading 6-12 months out |
| **$/MW or $/W installed cost trend** (renewables, manufacturers) | Declining vs peers; cost leadership | Flat/rising while peers decline | Cost-curve dynamics in cleantech are brutal — laggards die |
| **% contracted / hedged** (everything cyclical) | Sufficient to cover fixed obligations through 24 months of trough | Fully exposed at peak prices with no hedges | The single biggest determinant of whether the company controls its own destiny in the next downturn |

## Valuation framework

The **single most important rule:** apply the multiple appropriate to the sub-sector, not a generic energy multiple. EV/EBITDA on a refiner at peak crack spreads will look hilariously cheap and is almost always a value trap; the same multiple on a regulated utility is meaningless because most of the value is rate-base growth.

- **Primary multiple by sub-sector:** see navigator table above.
- **Secondary cross-checks (always run two):**
  - For commodity names: FCF yield at strip + at $X long-term price you actually believe in.
  - For E&P specifically: PV-10 / NAV at strip vs at long-term price assumption (this is where the "is the proved reserve real?" question lives).
  - For utilities/midstream: dividend yield vs 10Y treasury spread, plus FFO-based leverage.
  - For pre-profit cleantech: cost-curve trajectory + funded runway + FID'd project pipeline NPV.
- **Historical ranges (rough US-centric guides — pressure-test against current data):**
  - US E&P: EV/EBITDAX 3-7x, FCF yield 5-15% across the cycle. Outside this band on either side merits explanation.
  - Integrated majors: P/E 8-15x ex-trough; dividend yield 3-7%.
  - Midstream: EV/EBITDA 9-13x; distribution yield 5-9%.
  - US refiners: EV/EBITDA 4-8x mid-cycle (will look 2-3x at peak — the trap).
  - Regulated utilities: P/E 14-22x; premium/discount to S&P depends on rate-base growth; P/B 1.3-2.0x rate base.
  - YieldCos / renewables developers: P/AFFO 10-18x; highly sensitive to long rates.
  - Solar/wind manufacturers: highly variable; **gross margin trough is the right buy signal more often than the multiple**.
- **What drives the multiple up:** capital-discipline track record, contracted/regulated cash-flow proportion, low cost curve, balance-sheet headroom, jurisdictional quality, data-center exposure (currently), nuclear/LNG re-rating themes, organic inventory depth.
- **What drives the multiple down:** production-growth chasing, levered M&A, regulatory hostility, transition stranded-asset perceptions, subsidy cliffs, China dumping (cleantech), perception of capex creep, dependence on a single customer/jurisdiction.
- **When the multiple lies:**
  - **Refiners at peak crack spreads** — EV/EBITDA looks 3-4x and the trough is 6 months away.
  - **E&Ps with hedge books** that mask current realized prices — the unhedged forward picture is very different.
  - **Renewables YieldCos** where AFFO includes growth-driven AFUDC capitalization that doesn't generate cash.
  - **Cleantech equipment names** with deferred revenue from subsidy timing or one-time tax-credit transfers (post-IRA structures).
  - **Utilities** where the "rate base growth" is partially generation that's being depreciated faster than it's being recovered (regulatory disallowances coming).
  - **Pre-FCF growth stories** valued on EV/Sales where the unit economics don't pencil at scale (true of many hydrogen, fuel-cell, and battery names through history).
  - **Integrated majors at peak crude** — FCF yield looks 12-15% and the cycle is rolling.

## Red flags specific to this industry

The non-obvious ones — things that look fine in generic analysis but signal real problems here:

- **Production growth as the headline KPI.** Especially in E&P. Companies that lead with production growth (vs FCF/share, returns on capital, or cost-curve position) have historically destroyed value. The 2010s shale boom is the canonical case study — production grew dramatically; equity returns were negative.
- **Reserves replaced primarily through acquisition rather than the drillbit.** Sustainable E&P returns require organic reserve replacement at competitive F&D cost. Acquisition-led replacement is a hidden capex line and often value-destroying.
- **Distributions/dividends covered by issuance or debt.** Most prevalent in MLPs and YieldCos. If equity issuance is the bridge between distributions and underlying cash flow, the model is fragile and ends badly in any cost-of-capital shock.
- **"Energy transition pivots" with no execution track record.** A legacy oil company announcing a $20bn cleantech capex plan with no operating history in that sub-sector is a red flag, not a re-rating catalyst. Track record of project delivery in the **new** sub-sector matters more than the announced number.
- **Cleantech IRRs that require subsidies scheduled to phase out.** Many renewables and hydrogen models pencil only with current subsidy stacks. A change in administration, IRA rollback, or sunset clause can turn unit economics negative. **Always model unsubsidized economics.**
- **Solar/wind manufacturers competing with Chinese producers without a policy moat.** Western cleantech manufacturers without trade-protection or domestic-content premium pricing have repeatedly been crushed by Chinese cost dumping. Policy is the moat — or there isn't one.
- **Midstream contracts that look long-dated but have material recontracting cliffs.** "20-year contracts" can have rate resets, MVCs that step down, or counterparty credit risk that effectively shortens the duration. Read the actual contract structure, not the headline tenor.
- **Hedge book reclassification or aggressive hedge gain/loss treatment.** Energy companies with creative classification of hedging gains or losses (sometimes in operating, sometimes below the line) can flatter or hide volatility. **Demand the unhedged realized price.**
- **Successful efforts vs full cost accounting differences hiding write-downs.** US E&Ps using full cost accounting can defer impairment recognition compared to successful efforts peers. Comparing reported earnings across these without normalization is misleading.
- **Service-company "pricing power" claims at the top of a cycle.** Always wrong. The drillers, frackers, and big-3 services repeatedly tell investors the cycle is "different this time" near peaks. It isn't.
- **Utility rate cases pending in increasingly hostile jurisdictions.** A utility's bull case is regulatory constructiveness; if the state PUC has just installed activist commissioners or there's a backlash from prior rate increases, the rate-base growth assumption may be undeliverable.
- **Repeated impairments without management owning the prior decisions.** Serial impairments in E&P, refining, or merchant power without an explicit acknowledgment of capital-allocation error is a culture problem. The same decisions will recur.
- **Capex creep across multiple guidance updates.** Not a single overrun — the **pattern** of guidance drifting up over 3-4 quarters with no commensurate production/return improvement. This is how every up-cycle turns into a value-destruction phase.

## Common bull-case archetypes

The 2-3 stories that typically lead investors to overpay:

- **"Capital discipline is real this time."** True post-2020 for many US E&Ps; not universally true and prone to relapse at high prices. Pressure-test by looking at incremental capex behavior at the latest commodity peak — words are cheap, the latest budget is the test.
- **"AI / data-center power demand is structurally bullish for [gas / nuclear / utilities / grid tech]."** The thesis is real; the **magnitude and timing** are routinely overstated. Hyperscaler demand is concentrated in a handful of regions, gated by interconnection queues and transmission, and structurally lumpy. Buying utility/IPP names at premium multiples on this story without quantifying the actual incremental load to that specific company has been an expensive mistake before — and the 2024-2026 vintage of this trade is the highest-conviction-priced version yet.
- **"Energy transition supercycle / S-curve adoption."** Sometimes right (solar 2010-2020, EV 2018-2022). Often early. Adoption curves are non-linear and slope changes deliver 50%+ drawdowns even within secular winners. Multiple compression usually precedes fundamental disappointment.
- **"Stranded asset risk is overstated — oil demand has decades left."** The undersupply / underinvestment thesis. Often correct directionally but cyclical timing can be brutal. Validity depends on whether the specific company's reserves are competitive cost-curve assets — not whether oil demand is broadly resilient.
- **"Self-help cost-cutting story."** Watch for this in refining and integrated majors. Real cost-out exists; structural commoditization usually wins on a 5-year horizon.
- **"LNG buildout supercycle."** The Asian/European demand pull is real; the question is whether the specific terminal is contracted, its cost position vs Qatar/US Gulf, and whether the next wave of FIDs has already crashed the spread.

## Common bear-case archetypes

- **"Stranded assets / peak demand."** Real in places (high-cost coal, marginal oil sands without expansion runway, refineries in shrinking demand regions). Noise in others (low-cost Permian acreage, integrated majors with downstream/petchem optionality). The question is always: where on the global cost curve, and what's the asset half-life?
- **"Capital cycle turning."** When everyone has just announced multi-billion capex plans simultaneously, the cycle is rolling. Look at consensus capex 1-2 years out vs prior cycle peaks, and at industry-wide rig/capacity additions.
- **"Subsidy cliff."** Real when names depend on specific subsidy structures with sunset clauses or political vulnerability. Less risk for technologies that have crossed grid-parity thresholds in their geography.
- **"Inflation eating fixed-rate PPAs / contracts."** Hit YieldCos and renewables developers hard in 2022-2023; structurally relevant when long-dated cash flows are fixed but cost of capital and OPEX are floating.
- **"Cost-curve compression."** When marginal-cost production becomes structurally cheaper (US shale post-2014 vs OPEC; Chinese solar vs Western; Chinese batteries vs Western), the high-cost players don't recover.

## Capital allocation patterns

**How good operators deploy cash:**

- **Buy assets when they're cheap, sell or hold when they're expensive.** The CNQ playbook in oil sands; Equinor pre-2014 Norwegian deals; Cheniere's LNG build pre-2020. Test by examining M&A timing vs commodity price level historically — not just headline counts.
- **Return capital aggressively at the top of cycles.** Variable dividends (FANG, EOG when active), opportunistic buybacks during commodity strength, debt paydown when prices are high so the balance sheet is ready for the next trough.
- **Brownfield > greenfield > M&A** as a general capex hierarchy in mature sub-sectors. Brownfield expansion at incremental cost economics > new-build at full cost > acquired at peer pricing.
- **For utilities:** rate-base CAPEX where the regulatory framework is constructive, dividend growth aligned with EPS growth, equity issuance only at premium valuations, debt maturities matched to asset lives.
- **For midstream:** maintain coverage above 1.4x, fund growth with retained DCF + match-funded debt, no equity issuance into weak markets.
- **For cleantech:** fund the cost-curve descent, not market share. The companies that prioritized scale over profitability in solar/wind 2010-2018 mostly failed; the ones that prioritized cost leadership (FSLR among Western names; Chinese leaders) survived.

**Where bad operators destroy value:**

- Production-growth or capacity-growth chasing at the top of cycles.
- Levered, large M&A at peak prices (CHK 2014, multiple Permian deals 2014-2019, OXY/APC 2019 debated).
- Equity issuance to fund distributions (MLPs pre-2015).
- "Diversification" capex into sub-sectors where there's no operational track record.
- Buybacks at the top, dividend cuts at the bottom (the **inverse** of compounding).
- Capex creep — guidance slowly drifting up over multiple quarters with no commensurate production/return improvement.
- Hedge book restructuring that locks in low prices to "preserve cash flow" near the bottom of the cycle (the classic forced-hedge mistake).

## Mental-model crossover risks

If your other frameworks are software-heavy or consumer-facing, watch for these crossover mistakes when applying to energy:

- **"Scale = winner-take-most" doesn't hold here.** In commodity sub-sectors, the lowest-cost producer earns the cycle; scale is necessary but not sufficient and often becomes a liability when the cycle rolls (more fixed costs to amortize).
- **"Recurring revenue" doesn't mean "stable margins."** Utility revenue is recurring; merchant power revenue is recurring; but the spreads behind them are not, and the right test is contracted/regulated **cash-flow proportion**, not revenue stability.
- **Asset life is measured in decades, not product cycles.** Decisions made today play out over 20-50 years. Refineries built in the 1960s still operate; pipelines older than that. A "growth" capex commitment is a multi-decade obligation.
- **Working capital intensity is structurally higher** than tech — hydrocarbon inventory, project receivables, decommissioning liabilities. Free cash flow can be very different from EBITDA - capex.
- **"Network effects" mostly don't exist.** Pipelines have geographic monopolies (good). Most other sub-sectors don't have demand-side network economics. Don't import the framework.
