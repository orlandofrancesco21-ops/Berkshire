# US managed care & health services — evaluation framework

> Personal playbook for evaluating businesses that combine health insurance (commercial, Medicare Advantage, Medicaid, exchanges) with vertically integrated services (PBM, primary care, surgery centers, home health, analytics). Covers UnitedHealth Group, Elevance Health, Cigna, Humana, CVS Health, Centene, Molina, plus pure-play services adjacencies (HCA, Tenet, DaVita) where useful.
> Append-only. Refine every time you study a new name in this space.

**Last updated:** 2026-05-20

---

## What this industry is

US managed care is the business of underwriting healthcare risk for ~150 million Americans across four distinct customer pools — commercial employer-sponsored insurance, Medicare Advantage (privatized Medicare), Medicaid managed care, and ACA exchange individual plans — and progressively layering services (pharmacy benefits, primary care clinics, home health, surgery centers, claims/coding analytics, data and AI products) on top of the insurance chassis. Revenue comes from a fixed monthly per-member fee (the "premium" in commercial; "rate" or "capitation" in government programs) for which the insurer accepts the risk of total medical cost. The economics work when actuarial pricing accurately predicts medical costs and operational scale lets the insurer process claims, build provider networks, and negotiate drug prices cheaply enough to clear a 4-6% operating margin on revenue. The economics break when (a) medical costs accelerate faster than premium increases were priced for — the "medical loss ratio (MLR) blowout" pattern that dominates 2024-2026; (b) government program rates are cut by CMS or state Medicaid agencies; (c) the insurer's risk-coding practices come under regulatory scrutiny for upcoding; (d) member volume contracts faster than fixed-cost reduction can keep up. The single most important strategic shift of the last decade has been **vertical integration** — UnitedHealth's Optum, Cigna's Evernorth, CVS's Caremark+Aetna+Oak Street, Humana's CenterWell — moving the industry from "insurer that pays providers" to "integrated payer-provider-PBM platform that captures rent at multiple steps of the healthcare chain." Whether this integration is genuine value creation or rent-extraction that invites regulatory pushback is the single biggest unsettled question in the sector.

## Industry structure

- **Concentration:** highly concentrated. Top 5 managed care companies (UNH, ELV, CI, CVS, HUM) cover ~50% of US insured lives. Within Medicare Advantage, UnitedHealthcare and Humana together cover ~45% of MA members. Within commercial, UNH and ELV plus the regional Blues dominate. Within Medicaid, Centene, Molina, and UnitedHealth lead.
- **Customer:** triple-headed. The *insured member* is the nominal customer but rarely pays directly. The *plan sponsor* (employer for commercial; CMS for Medicare Advantage; state for Medicaid; HealthCare.gov for exchanges) is the economic customer who negotiates pricing and selects plans. The *broker/consultant channel* (Mercer, Aon, Lockton) influences ~70% of commercial mid-market business.
- **Cyclicality:** low to moderate. Commercial membership tracks employment with a lag. Medicare Advantage is secular-growth (aging population) but rate-cycle-sensitive. Medicaid is counter-cyclical (recessions push enrollment up). The mix smooths most of the headline cyclicality.
- **Capital intensity:** moderate. Insurance itself is capital-light (regulatory capital required but not heavy fixed assets). The services side has become capital-heavier — clinic networks, surgery centers, home health rollups all carry real PP&E and people overhead.
- **Operating leverage:** moderate. Most cost is variable medical cost (~80% of premium). The remaining 20% covers SG&A, broker commissions, and operating margin. A 200 bps MLR move flows almost dollar-for-dollar to operating income — the leverage is concentrated in the MLR line.
- **Regulatory regime:** **heavy and increasingly hostile**. CMS controls MA rates and STAR ratings; state Medicaid agencies set state rates; DOJ enforces antitrust on M&A and (rising importance) Medicare Advantage upcoding; FTC investigates PBM practices; SEC enforces disclosure on care utilization and reserve setting; HHS-OIG conducts ongoing audits; class action plaintiffs target prior-authorization denials. Regulatory risk is the dominant non-MLR variable.
- **Geographic concentration:** US-only by definition. State-by-state Medicaid concentration matters within the Medicaid-tilted names (Centene, Molina). MA penetration varies by county and matters at the regional level.

## What makes a great business in this industry

The 6-7 traits that distinguish compounders from also-rans:

1. **Scale advantage in claims processing and provider network leverage.** The single most durable cost advantage. The largest insurers process more claims more cheaply, negotiate larger network discounts with providers, and absorb regulatory compliance costs across more lives. UNH and ELV's scale advantage over Centene and Molina shows up in 100-200 bps of structural SG&A advantage that compounds quietly across decades.
2. **Vertical integration that creates real economics, not just optical revenue.** Optum, Evernorth, and Caremark each capture economics at the PBM, clinic, and analytics layers that pure-play insurers pay third parties for. Done right, this generates 30%+ operating margins on the services side (vs 4-6% on insurance) and provides earnings diversification. Done badly (some Optum Health rollups, some Walgreens-era CVS clinic acquisitions), it just adds capital-light revenue at zero or negative operating margin. **The test: does the services segment EBIT grow faster than its revenue?**
3. **Disciplined Medicare Advantage growth.** MA is the secular tailwind of the industry — aging population, government privatization preference, structurally good economics when STAR-rated 4+ stars. But MA is also where the regulatory risk concentrates (upcoding, prior-authorization denials, marketing practices). Operators that grow MA membership at "above 4-star quality, in counties with favourable rate parity" win the next 10 years. Operators that grow MA "by buying lower-margin contracts in adversely-selected counties" are accumulating future writedowns.
4. **Pricing discipline at renewals.** Commercial insurance is mostly annual renewal; MA is annual bid cycle; Medicaid is state-by-state contract cycle. The discipline to walk away from underpriced business — to lose membership rather than write at unprofitable rates — is the single most important operating habit. Operators that grow membership at any cost (Anthem 2017-2019, Centene various periods) tend to give it back later in MLR blowouts.
5. **Reserves and actuarial honesty.** Insurance accounting depends on management's estimate of incurred-but-not-reported (IBNR) claims at quarter-end. Operators that take reserves cautiously and surprise with positive prior-period development are the trustworthy compounders; operators that surprise with negative development quarter after quarter are signalling that pricing is chronically behind cost trend. Watch the prior-period development line specifically.
6. **Capital allocation discipline.** The industry has a long history of value-destructive M&A (CVS-Aetna at the peak, Cigna's Express Scripts deal, multiple managed-Medicaid rollups). The good operators (UNH historically until 2022, ELV in places, Humana in recent years) bias toward buybacks at low multiples and avoid transformative deals at high prices. Buybacks at low multiples are the highest-return use of capital in this industry, as in most.
7. **A regulatory posture that doesn't court enforcement.** This is harder to assess and increasingly important. Operators that aggressively maximize MA risk-coding revenue, push prior-auth denial rates to industry-leading levels, and lean on broker incentive structures to acquire low-quality enrollees are accumulating regulatory liability that compounds invisibly until a DOJ subpoena, class action, or rate cut crystallizes it. The healthiest operators run their compliance organization as a brake on growth, not as a window-dressing function.

## Key drivers of returns

What makes the **stock** work over a 5-year hold:

- **Medical cost trend vs premium pricing.** The single most important variable. Premium pricing is set ~12-18 months in advance; medical cost trend can accelerate or decelerate inside that window. The reflexive risk: when cost trend accelerates faster than pricing was set, MLR spikes and earnings cut as much as 30-50% in a single year (the 2024-2026 cycle is the canonical example).
- **Medicare Advantage rate cycle.** CMS announces MA rates ~9 months before the plan year. Constructive rates (above cost trend) drive multi-year earnings expansion; restrictive rates compress earnings 1-2 years out. The 2023-2026 rate cycle has been broadly restrictive; whether 2027-2029 normalizes is the most important sector-level catalyst.
- **Regulatory shock risk.** DOJ investigations into upcoding, FTC actions on PBMs, state-level rate cuts, plaintiff class actions — these are episodic but can cut 20%+ off a stock in a quarter when they materialize. Regulatory shocks have replaced underwriting cycles as the dominant single-name volatility source 2024-2026.
- **Operating margin trajectory by segment.** Insurance segments at 4-6% is the structural floor and ceiling; services segments at 6-12% (Optum Health) or 5-7% (PBM) is the marginal-dollar profit pool. Operators where the services segments are accelerating compound faster.
- **Per-share growth via buybacks at low multiples.** When the cycle turns down (2014-2015 for various names, 2024-2026 for UNH), valuation compression creates an asymmetric buyback opportunity. The operators that execute counter-cyclically (UNH historically until 2022) compound durably; those that buy back near peaks add per-share earnings that compress in the next downturn.
- **Multiple re-rate as "is the regulatory tail risk priced?" resolves.** This has dominated 2024-2026 for UNH specifically. If the DOJ resolves without structural penalties, the multiple expands; if it produces structural changes to MA economics, the multiple stays compressed indefinitely.

## Industry-specific KPIs

Generic financial ratios (P/E, ROE, FCF/revenue) are second-order here. The diagnostic metrics:

| Metric | What good looks like | What concerning looks like | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|---|
| Medical Loss Ratio (MLR / MCR) | 82-84% commercial; 85-87% MA at scale; 88-90% Medicaid | >86% commercial; >89% MA; sequential 100+ bps deterioration | The cleanest single test of underwriting health |
| Sequential MLR direction | Improving QoQ in plan year | Deteriorating mid-plan-year | Mid-year MLR move means pricing miss is structural |
| Prior-period development | Favourable or neutral | Repeatedly unfavourable | Catches reserve under-statement; classic late-cycle leading indicator |
| Days claims payable (DCP) | Stable or rising | Falling | Falling DCP can signal reserve release inflation |
| MA membership growth + STAR rating mix | 4-star+ growth; counties with constructive rates | 3.5-star-and-below growth | The MA pipeline determines 2-3 year earnings |
| Net commercial enrollment growth | Stable or modest growth | Material losses to competitors | Commercial is the highest-margin pool |
| Medicaid contract retention | High; minimal contract losses | Material contract losses or rate cuts | State Medicaid is volatile in a way commercial isn't |
| Operating margin by segment | Insurance 5-6%; PBM 4-5%; Services 7-12% | Insurance <4%, Services flat, PBM declining | Where the marginal dollar is being made |
| Services revenue growth vs operating income growth | OI growth ≥ revenue growth | Revenue growth without OI growth | Services rollups that don't generate operating income are revenue-padding |
| SG&A as % of revenue | Declining over time; <14% at scale | Rising | Scale advantage at work or not |
| Capital returns yield (buyback + dividend) | 4-6% at scale, sustained | Below 3%, or executed at peak multiples | Capital allocation discipline test |
| Regulatory headline density | Quiet; legal reserves stable | Multiple concurrent investigations; rising legal reserves | Regulatory risk is structural, not transitory |

## Valuation framework

This is a forward P/E industry. EBITDA multiples are less informative because most asset value is on the insurance balance sheet, not in operating PP&E. P/E on adjusted earnings is the primary lens; dividend yield and FCF yield are the cross-checks. **Beware:** adjusted EPS in this industry often excludes amortization of acquired intangibles (legitimate for some PBM/services deals, less legitimate for others) — always look at both GAAP and adjusted, and at the size of the difference.

- **Primary multiple:** forward P/E (12-24m out) on adjusted earnings.
- **Secondary cross-checks:** FCF yield, dividend yield, EV/EBITDA, P/E vs broader S&P, P/E relative to managed-care peer median.
- **Historical range:** UNH has traded 14-30x forward P/E over 15 years. Sector typically trades 12-22x. **The 2025-2026 reset has compressed UNH to ~22x forward 2026 (on artificially-depressed numerator)** — but at normalized 2028 EPS, the forward multiple is closer to 14-16x. The "trough multiple on trough earnings" trap is the active risk: at $393 and $17.75 expected 2026 EPS, the optical multiple is 22x but on the depressed bottom-of-cycle earnings.
- **What drives the multiple up vs down:** MA rate cycle, regulatory headline volatility, MLR trajectory, services-segment growth durability, capital allocation execution, relative to broader market sentiment.
- **When does the multiple lie?** (a) **Bottom-of-cycle EPS at the bottom of the multiple range** — managed care earnings can drop 30-40% in a bad year; the multiple compresses concurrently; the apparent valuation looks reasonable on TTM but the right denominator is normalized EPS, not TTM. (b) **MA-heavy operators trading "in line" with diversified peers** despite higher regulatory tail risk — Humana before 2024 was the canonical example. (c) **Operators trading at premium multiples on Optum-style services revenue that hasn't been tested for sustainable operating margin** — Optum Health 2021-2023 was the canonical example.

## Red flags specific to this industry

Things that look fine in generic analysis but signal real problems here:

- **Persistently unfavourable prior-period development.** Reserves are being released in lieu of earnings; the next quarter's "miss" is being financed by understating IBNR. This is the most reliable leading indicator of MLR pressure 2-4 quarters out.
- **MA membership growth concentrated in 3.5-star-or-below contracts.** Star ratings determine MA quality bonus payments; sub-4-star plans earn meaningfully less per member. Growth in low-star segments is earnings-destructive.
- **Aggressive risk-adjustment coding intensity.** When a managed-care operator's risk scores grow materially faster than industry, regulators eventually notice. The 2024-2026 DOJ investigations into UnitedHealth, Humana, Elevance specifically target this dynamic.
- **Services-segment revenue growth without operating income growth.** Optum Health 2022-2024, CVS Health Care Delivery, Humana CenterWell — when services revenue scales faster than services EBIT, the integration thesis is rhetorical, not real.
- **Rising prior-auth denial rates concurrent with rising regulatory and political scrutiny.** Insurers that ride prior-auth aggressively to manage MLR are accumulating reputational and class-action risk. The Brian Thompson assassination (December 2024) crystallized political and consumer hostility toward UnitedHealthcare specifically and the industry generally.
- **CEO/CFO/segment-head turnover concentrated in 12-18 months.** Healthcare insurance is a culture of operational continuity; rapid C-suite turnover signals real strategic disagreement at the top.
- **PBM rebate structures coming under regulatory or legislative pressure.** FTC and state-level PBM investigations target the rebate-retention model; legislation that requires rebate pass-through changes PBM economics overnight.
- **Heavy reliance on M&A to grow Optum-style segments.** Internally-grown clinic networks have different economics from rolled-up clinic networks. Operators that pay 1.5-2x services revenue multiples are pre-paying margin for years.

## Common bull-case archetypes

- **"The integrated payer-services model has a permanent margin advantage."** Partially true; the question is whether the regulatory ceiling on MA upcoding and PBM rebate retention permanently compresses that advantage. The 2024-2026 cycle is the live test.
- **"Aging population = MA secular tailwind."** True at the volume level; not necessarily true at the margin level if CMS rates compress in line with cost trend or if STAR ratings tighten.
- **"Trough multiple, trough earnings — easy double."** True when both reset simultaneously and the underlying franchise is intact. The 2014-2015 managed-care reset paid off this way. False when the franchise itself is degrading (Centene 2020-2022 was harder than the bulls expected).

## Common bear-case archetypes

- **"Medical cost trend has structurally re-accelerated."** When GLP-1s, gene therapies, behavioural health utilization, and outpatient pricing all compound at >5% trend simultaneously, the industry's premium-pricing engine can't keep up. This is the dominant 2024-2026 fear.
- **"MA economics are being structurally compressed by CMS."** The 2023-2026 rate-cut cycle plus tightening STAR rating thresholds plus risk-adjustment intensity restrictions have all worked in the same direction. If this is the new normal, the MA earnings power of the integrated players (UNH especially) is permanently lower.
- **"Regulatory penalties / DOJ resolution forces structural change."** The structural-change scenarios — forced PBM divestiture, MA rate clawback, prior-auth restriction — are episodic but real. The market discounts a probability of each.
- **"Services-segment integration thesis is rent-extraction, not value-creation."** If true, regulatory pressure on PBMs and integrated clinic networks compresses the very economics that justified premium multiples for UNH-style integration leaders.

## Capital allocation patterns

How good operators in this industry deploy cash:

- **Buybacks at low multiples + sustained dividend growth has been the playbook for the highest-TSR names** (UNH 2007-2022, HCA periodically, Cigna periodically). UNH ran 15 consecutive years of double-digit dividend increases through 2024.
- **Transformative M&A has destroyed value more often than created it.** CVS-Aetna ($69B at the top of the cycle, written down repeatedly); Cigna-Express Scripts (debated); Humana's attempted Cigna deal (collapsed 2023). The exceptions — UnitedHealth's Catamaran 2015 and various Optum bolt-ons until ~2020 — required deep operational integration and were small relative to market cap.
- **Bolt-on services M&A scales the integrated payer-services thesis when done at reasonable multiples** — Optum's primary care rollup, Cigna's specialty pharmacy, Humana's CenterWell. When the multiples paid exceed 1.5x revenue or 15x EBITDA, the integration thesis usually doesn't earn its cost of capital.
- **Counter-cyclical buybacks at MLR-blowout troughs is the single best capital allocation decision in the industry.** 2014-2015 UNH and others bought back stock at sub-15x P/E and rode the recovery to 25x+. The 2025-2026 cycle is testing whether the survivors (UNH, ELV) repeat the pattern or pull back to preserve cash against regulatory uncertainty.

## Mental-model crossover risks

Assumptions from other frameworks that **do not transfer** here:

- **"Scale wins."** Partially true (claims processing, network leverage), but **regulatory exposure scales with scale**, sometimes faster. UNH's scale creates the cost advantage AND the regulatory target.
- **"Recurring revenue is stable."** Premium revenue looks recurring but is annually re-priced; a single bad pricing cycle can compress earnings 30%+. Don't model managed care as bond-like.
- **"Network effects compound."** No real network effects. The closest analogue — provider networks attracting more members — is mostly a scale effect.
- **"Brand pricing power applies."** Almost nothing. Brand barely matters; benefit design, premium pricing, and network access do. Don't model managed care on consumer-brand archetypes.
- **"Cyclically cheap = buying opportunity."** Usually true on managed-care MLR cycles where the franchise is intact. **Less true when the cycle is driven by regulatory change** — regulatory compression doesn't mean-revert the way underwriting cycles do.
- **"Multiple compression on fear is the time to buy."** Mostly true historically. The 2024-2026 cycle is harder to read because the fear is partly justified by real regulatory and political shifts (DOJ, Thompson assassination, public backlash against prior-auth). Don't assume mean reversion happens on the historical timeline.

- 2026-05-20 (UNH deep-dive, source: framework-bootstrap + initial dive): The most diagnostic single signal in the UNH 2024-2026 reset is that **Berkshire Hathaway bought UNH in Q2 2025 (revealed August 2025) and completely exited the position by Q1 2026 (revealed in 13F filed May 15, 2026)** — a roughly 9-month round trip in a famously slow-moving portfolio. Possible interpretations: (a) the rally from $270 to $400 closed the margin of safety they'd identified; (b) regulatory diligence revealed structural concerns the initial position hadn't fully accounted for; (c) opportunistic capital reallocation to better setups. In all cases, the smartest known long-only investor sized out at near-current prices. **Lesson: in any name where a respected disciplined investor has both entered and exited inside a short window, examine the price gap they exited at — it's often a more useful anchor than analyst targets.**
