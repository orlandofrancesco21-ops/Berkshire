# Creative & design software — evaluation framework

> Personal playbook for evaluating businesses whose economics depend on owning a creation/design workflow that professionals (designers, video editors, marketers, architects, engineers) live in for hours per day. Covers Adobe (Creative Cloud, Document Cloud, Experience Cloud), Autodesk (CAD/BIM), Figma (collaborative product design), Canva (mass-market design), Affinity / Procreate (challenger apps), and adjacencies like ANSYS, PTC, Bentley.
> Append-only. Refine every time you study a new name in this space.

**Last updated:** 2026-05-20

---

## What this industry is

Creative & design software businesses sell the digital tools that knowledge workers use to make things — visual content (Photoshop, Illustrator, Premiere, Lightroom), documents (Acrobat), product/UX designs (Figma, XD), 3D/CAD (Maya, AutoCAD, Inventor, Fusion 360), mass-market visuals (Canva), and the supporting infrastructure (Substance, Marvelous Designer, fonts, stock libraries). Revenue is overwhelmingly subscription-based (post the 2012-2018 industry-wide pivot from perpetual licenses to SaaS) and falls into three customer tiers: **consumer/prosumer** (single-seat creators, photographers, hobbyists — $20-80/mo); **SMB** (small studios, freelancers, marketing agencies — $50-150/seat/mo); and **enterprise** (Fortune 500 marketing departments, design agencies, automotive/aerospace engineering teams — $100s-$1000s/seat/year, often consolidated into ELAs running $10M+). Demand grows with the number of digital creators (TikTok creators, indie game devs, e-commerce sellers, 3D designers) and with marketing spend digitalization (every brand needs more variants of more assets for more channels). The economics work spectacularly when the software becomes the workflow standard (Photoshop verbed; AutoCAD became "drawing"; Figma became the design file shared with developers) because file format lock-in plus muscle memory in trained workers creates switching costs that survive a generation of CEOs. The economics fail when a new architecture (web-native multiplayer; AI-native generation) makes the standard look obsolete — at which point the installed base buys time but not destiny.

## Industry structure

- **Concentration:** mostly category-monopoly or duopoly within each workflow. Adobe owns commercial creative (~70%+ share in pro segments) and PDF/document; Autodesk owns mainstream CAD/BIM; Figma owns collaborative product design (post-Adobe-deal collapse, this is now a public independent); Canva owns mass-market design. Cross-category competition is rising fast (Canva moving up; Figma moving sideways into Slides/Whiteboard; Adobe moving into mass-market with Express). AI-native upstarts (Runway, Krea, MidJourney, ElevenLabs, Suno) are creating new categories rather than displacing existing ones — yet.
- **Customer:** ~40-50% consumer/prosumer, ~25-30% SMB, ~25-30% enterprise (Adobe Digital Experience is overwhelmingly enterprise). Geographic split is ~50% North America, ~30% EMEA, ~20% APAC.
- **Cyclicality:** mildly cyclical. Subscription revenue is sticky; net retention rates above 100% are normal for the well-run names. Enterprise software has some cyclicality through marketing-budget compression in recessions, but consumer creative is less cyclical than the broader economy — creators don't unsubscribe in volume.
- **Capital intensity:** structurally light. R&D is the major investment, typically 18-25% of revenue. Capex (datacenters, cloud infra, offices) is in the 3-5% of revenue range for the larger names. Working capital is favourable — subscriptions billed annually create durable negative working capital.
- **Operating leverage:** high. Once a product is built, marginal seats are nearly free. Best-in-class operators run 35-45% GAAP operating margins (non-GAAP often 45-50%+). Margin expansion is the dominant driver of EPS growth when revenue growth slows.
- **Regulatory regime:** light-touch historically; **antitrust scrutiny on M&A has risen sharply**. The Adobe-Figma deal collapse (Dec 2023, $1B termination fee) signalled that horizontal consolidation in creative software faces real regulatory resistance. AI training data and IP litigation (NYT vs OpenAI-style cases) are the emerging legal exposure.
- **Geographic concentration:** US-led. R&D centers, customer base, and pricing power skew heavily US/Western. Chinese market access has been structurally limited by piracy historically and is even more limited now by geopolitics.

## What makes a great business in this industry

The 6-7 traits that separate compounders from also-rans:

1. **Workflow lock-in expressed as file format.** The most durable software moats in this category are not features — they're file standards. .PSD, .AI, .DWG, .FIG, .PDF survive product cycles, executive turnover, and competitive challenge because the installed base of files locked in the format means switching means losing access to your own historical work. The single most predictive question about any creative software franchise: **does this company own a file format that's effectively a verb?**
2. **Muscle memory of trained users.** A senior designer who has spent 15 years in Photoshop has ~10,000 hours of keyboard shortcuts, plugin workflows, and team conventions in that one tool. The retraining cost is real and it accrues quarterly across the entire user base. This is what makes "best tool wins" largely false in mature creative categories — the incumbent tool wins by default unless the challenger is dramatically better, not marginally.
3. **Enterprise distribution moat (where applicable).** For enterprise-tilted names (Adobe Digital Experience, Autodesk, ANSYS), the enterprise sales motion — multi-year ELAs, IT integrations, vendor approval lists, procurement relationships — is itself an asset that takes 5-10 years to build and is hard for upstarts to replicate. Figma went enterprise from the ground up; Canva is struggling to scale into enterprise; Adobe and Autodesk own it.
4. **Pricing power within the workflow.** Best-in-class operators raise per-seat prices 5-10% per year for years without meaningful churn. Adobe Creative Cloud has roughly doubled in price 2013-2025 and grown subscribers. The ability to push price annually without losing the customer is the cleanest test of franchise quality.
5. **A credible R&D engine that ships big things.** Branded creative software is a constant arms race. The companies that fail aren't usually the ones that miss small features — they're the ones that miss architecture shifts (perpetual → SaaS; desktop → web; raster → AI-native; single-player → multiplayer/real-time collaboration). The next architecture shift is generative AI; how each incumbent positions itself within that shift will sort the next decade's winners and losers.
6. **Net dollar retention above 110%.** For enterprise-tilted businesses, NDR (existing-customer revenue this year vs same customer last year) is the single most diagnostic operational metric. Above 110% means the customer cohort is expanding seats and adopting more products. Below 100% means the franchise is shrinking inside its installed base — usually a leading indicator of structural decline.
7. **Capital allocation discipline through hype cycles.** Software has periodic hype cycles (cloud migration 2014-2018; remote-work pull-forward 2020-2021; AI 2023-?) where multiples and ambition both expand. Operators who buy back stock at peak multiples (Salesforce 2021, Adobe 2021-2022) or do dilutive M&A at peak comparables (many cases) destroy meaningful value. Operators who use cheap stock periods to buy back aggressively (Microsoft 2009-2014, Adobe 2024-2026) create it.

## Key drivers of returns

What makes the **stock** work over a 5-year hold:

- **The architecture-shift narrative.** When the market believes the incumbent is on the right side of the next architectural shift, multiples expand. When the market believes the incumbent is being disrupted, multiples compress — often violently. Cloud migration narrative drove Adobe's 2014-2020 re-rate; AI-disruption narrative drove the 2024-2026 de-rate.
- **Revenue growth durability.** The cleanest cross-sectional driver. Same multiple on accelerating revenue is wildly more valuable than declining revenue.
- **Operating margin trajectory.** Software companies trade on operating margin direction more than absolute level. A 35% margin going to 40% earns the stock; a 38% margin going to 33% loses it.
- **Net dollar retention.** Public NDR disclosures (where available) move multiples in real time. Below 110% in enterprise SaaS is now widely considered a yellow flag.
- **Pricing power demonstration.** Each successful price increase that doesn't trigger churn validates the moat. Each failed/rolled-back price increase erodes it.
- **Capital allocation execution.** Buybacks at low multiples create per-share growth that compounds invisibly; M&A done well is a tailwind; M&A done badly is a 5-year overhang.
- **Multiple as a function of "is the moat intact?"** For category-leader software, multiple compression on AI-disruption narrative has been the dominant 2023-2026 force. Re-rate higher requires demonstrated AI monetization that exceeds AI cannibalization.

## Industry-specific KPIs

Generic financial ratios mostly work here — software is a numerate industry — but these are the diagnostic metrics that matter most:

| Metric | What good looks like | What concerning looks like | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue growth (constant currency) | 12%+ for incumbents at scale; 25%+ for category insurgents | Sub-8% for incumbents; <15% for insurgents claiming hyper-growth | The most reliable predictor of multiple direction |
| ARR / NDR | NDR 110%+ | NDR <100% sustained | Net dollar retention is the inside view on whether customers are expanding or shrinking |
| Operating margin (non-GAAP) | 35%+ at scale, expanding | Sub-25% sustained or compressing | Operating leverage at work or not |
| Operating margin (GAAP) | 25%+ at scale; substantial SBC adjustment to non-GAAP is a flag | <15% sustained, especially if SBC >15% of revenue | The honest profitability test — SBC is real dilution |
| FCF / Revenue | 25-30%+ at scale | Sub-15% | Capital-light test; software that doesn't convert to FCF is suspicious |
| FCF conversion (FCF / non-GAAP net income) | 100%+ | Sub-80% | Catches financial engineering / aggressive non-GAAP adjustments |
| R&D / Revenue | 18-25%; trending stable | Below 15% (under-investing) or above 30% sustained without revenue follow-through (mis-firing) | The arms-race spend that determines the next architecture |
| S&M / Revenue | Declining over time as franchise matures | Rising while revenue growth slows (paying to maintain growth) | Distribution efficiency / brand strength |
| % of revenue from AI-adjacent products | Growing in absolute terms and as % | Flat or shrinking AI ARR while market AI ARR grows | The most-watched 2024-2026 sub-KPI |
| ROIC (ex-goodwill) | 30%+ | <15% | The capital-light test that catches over-spent M&A |
| Share count YoY | Flat or declining | Rising materially (SBC > buybacks) | The "is buyback policy net-net or just SBC-offset?" question |

## Valuation framework

This is a **forward P/E** and **EV/Sales** industry, with **FCF yield** as the income-style cross-check for mature names and **rule of 40** (revenue growth + operating margin %) as the catch-all for growth stage. EBITDA multiples are less informative than for capital-heavy industries because D&A is small relative to operating income.

- **Primary multiple:** forward P/E (12-24m out) for mature, profitable names (Adobe, Autodesk, Microsoft software segment). EV/Sales for high-growth not-yet-profitable names (Figma early-public, Canva when public). Rule of 40 as the catch-all.
- **Secondary cross-checks:** FCF yield (high relevance for mature SaaS); EV/FCF; EV/ARR; rule of 40 trend.
- **Historical range:** mature category-leaders typically 22-40x forward P/E; insurgents 30-80x EV/Sales early then compressing as they mature. **The 2024-2026 AI-fear environment has pushed mature creative software to 10-15x forward P/E** — bottom-decile of 15-year ranges — which is the cheapest the category has been since 2009.
- **What drives the multiple up vs down:** revenue growth direction, AI narrative, net dollar retention, operating margin trajectory, pricing actions taken, M&A activity, competitive losses (e.g. Figma vs Adobe XD), and macro software-sector sentiment.
- **When does the multiple lie?** (a) **Mature SaaS at "trough" multiples on peak revenue growth** — if the revenue growth was the peak and decelerates structurally, the multiple isn't actually low. (b) **Insurgent SaaS at "growth" multiples on still-unproven retention** — high revenue growth from new logos with mediocre NDR is a classic 12-month-out value trap. (c) **Multiples that look reasonable on non-GAAP that fall apart on GAAP** — heavy SBC users where the dilution adjustment is structural, not transitory.

## Red flags specific to this industry

Things that look fine in generic analysis but signal real trouble here:

- **Net dollar retention falling below 105%.** Even when revenue growth still looks healthy, NDR is the leading indicator. Customers stop expanding seats 2-4 quarters before they leave.
- **Heavy reliance on price increases (not volume) for growth.** A franchise that's growing only through annual price hikes while net-new-seat growth stalls is on the slow path to peak-revenue decline. The healthiest growth is mix of seat growth + price + product expansion.
- **Discounting at renewal (especially in enterprise).** When you start hearing of ELA renewals signed below list pricing or with shrinking commitments, the moat is leaking inside the installed base.
- **An "AI strategy" that's mostly free features.** Real AI monetization means new SKUs, new credits, or new tiers customers pay extra for. Free features bolted onto existing subscriptions are the defensive play, not the value-creating one.
- **SBC consistently exceeding buybacks.** A company that "returns $5B in buybacks" but issues $3B in SBC has net-returned $2B, not $5B. Many software names fail this test more than the IR deck implies.
- **A failed M&A attempt followed by acquisition of a less attractive target.** The Adobe-Figma collapse is the canonical case — when an attempted acquisition is blocked, the impulse to "do something" can lead to lower-quality deals. Watch what management does in the 18 months following a blocked deal.
- **Senior product/engineering departures concentrated in one segment.** Software companies don't fail uniformly — they fail one workflow at a time. Watch for engineering leadership churn in specific product lines.
- **Architecture-shift denial.** When management is explicit that "the old architecture is still fine, our customers prefer it" while challengers are growing fast on the new architecture, the incumbency thesis is the bear case. This was Oracle in 2008-2014 vs cloud; it may be parts of Adobe in 2024-2026 vs AI-native creation.

## Common bull-case archetypes

The stories that typically lead investors to overpay:

- **"The category leader's moat is too deep to disrupt."** True for some workflows over 5-year horizons; false over 10-year horizons when the architecture shifts (Microsoft Office and Excel survived; Microsoft FrontPage and Encarta didn't). The general principle: workflow lock-in is real but architecture shifts are real too, and they're not symmetric — incumbents lose more on architecture shifts than they gain by maintaining lock-in.
- **"AI is a tailwind because the leader will monetize it from the installed base."** Partially true; depends on whether the AI value accrues to the workflow owner or to the AI infrastructure provider (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google) and on whether AI-native tools build new installed bases faster than incumbents can defend.
- **"Net retention >110% means the franchise compounds."** True until it isn't — NDR is a coincident or slightly-leading indicator, not a guarantee. When it rolls over, it rolls over fast.
- **"Software margins always expand."** Not always. Saturation, increased competition for talent, and AI-related cloud-compute costs are all margin headwinds.

## Common bear-case archetypes

The stories that typically signal genuine trouble vs noise:

- **"AI-native challengers are growing share of new creator cohorts."** When new entrants to the category (first-time photographers, first-time designers, first-time 3D modelers) skip the incumbent and start on an AI-native or web-native alternative, the long-term replacement cohort is being lost. The framework's "muscle memory" moat works only on people already trained — it doesn't help with the next generation.
- **"Pricing power is gone."** When the incumbent stops raising prices, or raises them and rolls back, the moat is degrading whether or not revenue has rolled over yet.
- **"The incumbent's response to architecture shift is a bolt-on, not a re-architecture."** A meaningful sign of bear case in the next architecture shift.
- **"Wholesale (re)architecture by management without revenue follow-through."** Conversely, big pivots without monetization 12-24 months in are a sign that the strategy hasn't worked.

## Capital allocation patterns

How good operators in this industry deploy cash:

- **Aggressive buybacks at low multiples are the highest-return use of capital.** Microsoft 2009-2014, Oracle 2013-2018, Adobe 2024-2026 (running). The companies that bought back stock at 30-40x P/E in 2020-2021 (Salesforce most notoriously) destroyed shareholder value at the same time as their IR decks were celebrating "capital returns."
- **Dividend policy is variable.** Adobe, Autodesk, Figma — no dividend. Microsoft and Oracle — yes. The industry default is to prioritize buybacks given high FCF growth; dividend initiation is usually a maturity signal.
- **M&A: bolt-ons usually fine; transformative deals dangerous.** Microsoft has made M&A work (LinkedIn, GitHub, Activision); Adobe's Macromedia (2005) worked, Magento/Marketo (2018) were debated, Figma was blocked. Default to scrutiny on any deal above 10% of market cap.
- **Stock-based comp is structural — be specific about net of SBC capital returns.** Software is the highest-SBC industry; the gross buyback number minus SBC issuance is the true capital return.
- **R&D as capex.** R&D is in operating expense but functionally is the capex of a software business — the long-duration investment that produces future revenue. Names that under-invest in R&D to flatter margins are the canonical decay trap.

## Mental-model crossover risks

Assumptions from other frameworks that **do not transfer** here:

- **"Recurring revenue is stable."** Mostly true, but with two crucial caveats: **architecture shifts can collapse the installed base over 5-10 years** (mainframe → client-server, desktop → cloud, current → AI-native), and **NDR declines are leading indicators, not lagging.** Don't model SaaS as the bond-like stability that some investors assume.
- **"Brand pricing power applies."** Software pricing power comes from workflow lock-in, not brand love. The mechanism is different from consumer brands — a designer doesn't "love" Photoshop, they're locked in. Don't model creative software pricing power on consumer-brand archetypes.
- **"Scale wins."** Mostly true within a category, but **architecture shifts redistribute the category** — and scale in the old architecture is sometimes a millstone, not an advantage. Microsoft's mobile failure happened despite scale; Kodak's digital failure happened despite scale.
- **"Network effects compound."** Genuine network effects in creative software are rarer than claimed. Figma has them (shared design files). Photoshop largely doesn't (one designer's files don't make another designer's tool more valuable, beyond file-format compatibility). Don't over-credit network effects when assessing moats.
- **"Cyclically cheap = buying opportunity."** Often true for category-leader software, but the bear case here is **structurally cheap, not cyclically cheap** — multiples can stay compressed for years if the AI-disruption narrative holds. Don't confuse multiple compression on macro fear (correct buying opportunity) with multiple compression on structural disruption (potential value trap).
- **"Insurgent disruption follows the disruption-theory playbook (bottom-up).** AI disruption in creative software is **both** bottom-up (Canva eating the entry-level) and top-down (Runway and Sora threatening high-end video). The clean Christensen playbook doesn't apply; assess the disruption from both ends.
