## What the business does

Nike designs and markets athletic footwear, apparel, and accessories under the Nike, Jordan, and Converse brands, sold globally through ~30,000 wholesale doors (Foot Locker, Dick's, JD Sports, etc.), through ~1,000 owned Nike retail stores, and through its own digital channels (Nike.com, SNKRS app, training apps). Manufacturing is fully outsourced — primarily to Vietnam (~50%), Indonesia (~25%), and China (~15%), with about 30 first-tier contract manufacturers producing all footwear under Nike specifications. The company spends roughly $5B/year on demand creation (marketing, athlete endorsements, sponsorships) to maintain the brand's cultural permission to charge premium prices. Roughly 65% of revenue comes from footwear, 28% from apparel, the balance from equipment and Converse. Geographic split is approximately 42% North America, 27% EMEA, 14% Greater China, 13% APAC/LatAm, 4% global brand divisions.

## The bull case

Three framework-grounded points:

1. **The franchise is intact and the turnaround is real.** Elliott Hill (CEO since October 2024, 32-year Nike veteran who came out of retirement) inherited a company that had over-rotated to DTC and starved wholesale. By Q3 FY26 (Feb 2026 quarter), wholesale revenue grew 5% reported with North America wholesale up 11% — the first quarter where the rebuild was clearly visible in numbers. Nike is back on Amazon (after exiting 2019), has rebuilt Foot Locker and Dick's relationships, and is investing $5B in 2026 marketing — a shift from promo-heavy clearance back to brand-building. The framework's #1 trait ("cultural permission to charge") is dented, not destroyed. The Jordan brand alone is a multi-billion-dollar franchise that nobody else in the world can replicate. The bull case is that within 24-36 months, the wholesale rebuild produces sell-through, sell-through produces inventory normalization, normalization produces gross margin recovery from ~40% back toward 45-46%, and EPS compounds from a depressed ~$1.50 base back toward $3.50-4.00.

2. **Valuation reflects severe pessimism.** Nike trades at ~$43 against a 10-year revenue/EPS history showing peak EPS of $3.75 (FY22). On TTM EPS of $1.53, the stock is at ~28x P/E — but on normalized FY28 earnings of $3.00-3.50, the stock is at 12-14x forward two-year — bottom-decile of Nike's own historical range. FCF yield at ~5%, dividend yield 3.5%+ (top of 10-year range), $5B+ of net cash, $18B authorized buyback program with ~$6B remaining. Capital allocation discipline appears to have improved — Hill has slowed buybacks at current levels relative to the 2021-2022 pace when management bought at peak multiples. Even at lower buyback velocity, the share count is steadily declining and dividend coverage is uncontested. The framework's bull-case archetype warning ("the brand is forever — buy on dips") applies, but it doesn't make the archetype wrong every time — it makes it wrong in the cases where the brand truly has lost cultural permission. The base-rate question is whether Nike's situation looks more like Under Armour 2017 (real, permanent erosion) or Adidas 2022-2023 (sharp dislocation, multi-year recovery). Both elements of Nike are present, which is why pricing has compressed; but the franchise depth (Jordan especially) argues for the Adidas-2023 reference class.

3. **The Hill / wholesale-rebuild playbook compounds even if Nike never regains insurgent levels of share.** A more disciplined Nike that captures 28-30% of the global athletic footwear market (down from ~32% at peak) and runs at 12% operating margin (down from 13.5% peak) — i.e. permanently somewhat smaller and somewhat less profitable than the old Nike — still generates $4B+ of FCF/year and earns its current market cap back in <16 years on fundamentals alone, ignoring multiple recovery. The framework warns that the bull case "the brand is forever" is a trap; the structural under-write here doesn't require the brand to be forever, only that it stabilises at a smaller, more disciplined, still-profitable version of itself. **The structural floor is materially above current valuation.**

## The bear case

Three framework-grounded points. Steelmanned:

1. **The brand has permanently lost a generation of consumers in two of its most important categories.** In US running specialty, Brooks and Hoka are now tied for category leadership at ~23% share each — Nike is no longer the obvious default. In Greater China, Nike revenue is down 10-17% per quarter through FY26 with online declines worse than physical (the framework's red flag about online leading physical — directly observed). Local brands Anta and Li-Ning have credibly competed on product, distribution, and cultural relevance with Chinese consumers. The framework's bear archetype "the brand has lost the kids" has been validated in surveys: middle and high-school cohort preference for Nike has declined materially since 2021, with Hoka, On, and Adidas (Sambas) picking up the share. This is the canonical pattern that preceded Under Armour's permanent impairment — and Under Armour was a much weaker franchise to begin with. If the cultural decline is the dominant narrative, current 18-20x forward P/E is barely justified and "cheap" Nike at $43 could become "expensive" Nike at $32-35 over the next 18 months.

2. **Operating margin recovery is slower and shallower than the bull thesis assumes.** Nike's Q3 FY26 gross margin came in at 40.2%, down 130 bps YoY, with management guiding to continued pressure into FY27 from (a) clearance markdowns to normalize inventory, (b) ~$1.5B FY26 tariff headwind that is partly structural, (c) Greater China product mix erosion, (d) elevated marketing spend ($5B+) without proportional revenue lift. The framework specifies that operating margin sub-8% sustained is the signal of brand erosion, not just a tough year — Nike's TTM operating margin is approximately 9% and the trajectory is sideways. If FY27 operating margin lands at 8-9% rather than the consensus 10-11%, FY28 earnings come in $2.50 not $3.50, and the bull-case valuation falls apart. The framework's "trough multiples on peak earnings" red flag is precisely the trap: Nike's stock looks cheap on TTM and on consensus FY27, but consensus may be modelling a faster recovery than is realistic.

3. **Even a successful Nike turnaround leaves the structural ceiling lower.** Three things have permanently changed: (a) Greater China is structurally impaired for foreign brands and unlikely to return to 2021 peak in our lifetime; (b) the insurgents (On, Hoka) have permanently taken share in running, which means the global TAM Nike can capture is structurally smaller; (c) tariff levels have settled at a meaningfully higher steady state, costing 150-250 bps of gross margin durably. Each of these alone is manageable; together they argue that Nike's normalized earnings power is closer to $3.00 than to the $4.00+ that some bulls model. At 20x $3.00, fair value is ~$60 — only 40% above current, with significant execution risk to even get there over 2-3 years. The reward is real but the asymmetry is less attractive than at first glance.

## Capital allocation track record

Five-year capital allocation data (FY21-FY25, fiscal years ending late May):

| Year | FCF (~$B) | Buybacks ($B) | Dividends ($B) | M&A | Capex |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| FY21 | ~5.8 | 0.6 | ~1.6 | none material | ~$0.7B |
| FY22 | ~4.4 | 4.0 | ~1.8 | none material | ~$0.8B |
| FY23 | ~5.5 | 5.0 | ~2.0 | none material | ~$1.0B |
| FY24 | ~6.6 | 4.3 | ~2.2 | none material | ~$0.8B |
| FY25 | ~3.3 | 3.0 | ~2.4 | none material | ~$0.8B |

**Read:** mixed. The buyback execution was uneven — Nike repurchased aggressively in FY22-FY24 when the stock was at $130-170, well above today's $43 price. That's $13B+ of buybacks at average prices that are now optically poor. By the framework's standard ("buybacks at trough valuations are the highest-return use of capital"), the pre-Hill buyback program was poor capital allocation — the company bought near the top of its own cycle. **However**, dividend growth has been disciplined (23 consecutive years of increases, ~8% per year for the last decade), capex has remained light (1-2% of revenue), and the company has avoided destructive M&A. M&A discipline is genuinely a Nike strength — past mistakes (Umbro, Cole Haan, Hurley — all divested at losses) appear to have made the organization permanently skeptical of acquisitions. **On a forward basis**, Hill has slowed buybacks (FY25 down 30%), which is the right call at current prices given uncertainty — though the framework's strongest signal would be aggressive buybacks at $40-45 if conviction is high. So far, capital is being preserved rather than deployed counter-cyclically — defensible given the turnaround uncertainty, but not yet what would qualify as great capital allocation. **Net: capital allocation track record is "fine but not great," with the most recent FY25 discipline being a positive signal.**

## Management quality

- **Elliott Hill (CEO, October 2024–present).** 32-year Nike veteran. Started in sales 1988, retired 2020 as President of Consumer & Marketplace. Hill is a culture-and-distribution operator — exactly the resume profile needed to repair the wholesale damage and re-energize the product-marketing-athlete flywheel. The fact that he came out of retirement at age 60+ to take the job suggests genuine institutional commitment rather than a career-stepping-stone CEO. Transcripts (Q1-Q3 FY26) read as direct, specific, and honest about what was broken — calls out wholesale neglect by name, names competitors who took share, doesn't hide behind macro. Compensation skewed heavily to long-dated equity tied to multi-year revenue and ROIC targets — alignment is reasonable but not exceptional.
- **John Donahoe (CEO, 2020-2024).** The architect of the DTC overshoot. Came from eBay/ServiceNow background — a tech operator, not a brand operator. The framework's "the CEO is fighting the last war" bear-case archetype applied — Donahoe imported tech-platform thinking into a brand business and got the strategic mix wrong. Tenure ended in October 2024 with a "retirement" announcement that read as a board-driven removal.
- **Phil Knight (Co-founder, Chairman Emeritus).** Still influential at the board level. His son Travis Knight sits on the board. The founder presence at the institutional level is a positive — provides a brand-instinct check on management.
- **Reading transcripts (Q1-Q3 FY26).** Hill speaks plainly about specific products, specific athletes, specific store partnerships. Less corporate, more operational. CFO Matt Friend is steady and specific on numbers. Quality of disclosure is high relative to peers — Nike segments Greater China clearly, breaks out wholesale vs DTC, gives directional guidance on tariff impact.

**Read:** management quality is materially better post-Hill than under Donahoe. Whether it's "great" depends on results 18-24 months out. The biographical fit between Hill's resume and the problem is the most encouraging single signal.

## What would change my mind

**Bull-case kill switches (signals that would invalidate the bull thesis):**

- North America wholesale revenue growth turns negative again for 2+ consecutive quarters (signals the wholesale rebuild is failing).
- Foot Locker / Dick's reports underperforming Nike sell-through vs Adidas/Hoka/On for 2+ consecutive quarters (signals the consumer isn't responding even as wholesale partners give Nike shelf space back).
- Gross margin compresses below 40% and stays there for 3+ quarters (signals brand erosion is structural, not transitory).
- Operating margin sub-8% sustained for FY27 (signals the framework's "brand erosion" threshold has been crossed).
- A material athlete defection — losing a top-tier basketball or running endorsement to a competitor — that signals the cultural pipeline is broken at the elite level.
- Greater China revenue declines accelerating beyond -20% YoY (signals the structural impairment is worsening, not stabilising).
- Inventory builds again (DIO rising for 2+ quarters) — the framework's #1 forward indicator failing.
- Management starts talking about another strategic pivot or buys an acquisition — signals the turnaround thesis is being abandoned.

**Bear-case kill switches (signals that the bear case is wrong and the franchise is back):**

- Gross margin returns above 44% on a 4-quarter trailing basis (signals pricing power is restored).
- Sell-through at Foot Locker / Dick's outpaces category growth for 3+ consecutive quarters (signals the consumer is back with Nike).
- A clear hero-product hit — a 2026-2027 launch that goes culturally viral and sells through at full price across multiple geographies. Equivalent to what Sambas did for Adidas in 2023-2024.
- Greater China revenue returns to flat or modest growth (signals at least cyclical stabilization).
- Nike running specifically wins back share — a new credible super-shoe / training shoe / lifestyle running franchise that takes meaningful share back from Hoka and On.
- Operating margin trajectory inflects upward (Q-over-Q sequential improvement for 3+ quarters).

## Conviction

**Level (1-5):** 3 (moderate)

**Why this level:** The franchise quality is real, the price discount is real, and the management upgrade is real. But the bear case has genuine teeth — Greater China is structural, the insurgents are not going away, and Nike's gross margin recovery is taking longer than consensus models. The asymmetry is favourable but not exceptional: rough estimate is 50-70% upside to $65-75 over 2-3 years in the base case, vs 25-30% downside to $30-35 in the bear case. The asymmetry is good (roughly 2:1) but not great (3:1+ would push conviction to 4). I'd want to see one more quarter of wholesale sell-through data and a more concrete sign of gross margin stabilization before going to higher conviction.

## Position sizing logic

A name with the franchise depth of Nike at this level of discount earns a starting position. Recommended sizing: **3-4% initial position**, with capacity to add to **5-6%** on either (a) a clear margin-inflection data point or (b) further price weakness toward $35-38 without thesis deterioration. The case for going larger would be a second sell-through data point confirming the bear case is wrong; that would justify 6-7%. The case for smaller is execution risk — Nike is one of those names where the bull case "is so obvious" that the consensus may already largely embed it at current prices, leaving less asymmetry than the numbers suggest. The framework's reminder applies: branded businesses can look cheap on the way down for years before they bottom. Don't size to "this can't go lower." It can.

**Action implied:** if the price held this level, a starting 3-4% BUY would be defensible today on conviction-3 basis. Higher conviction warrants more size and would be supported by 1-2 more quarters of data.
