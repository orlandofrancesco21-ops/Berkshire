# Branded athletic footwear & apparel — value chain

## Value chain map

Raw materials → Component manufacturing → Finished-goods manufacturing → Brand owner (design, marketing, distribution) → Wholesale aggregator → Consumer.

- **Raw materials.** Synthetic textiles, rubber, EVA foam, midsole compounds (proprietary in some cases — Nike's React, Adidas Boost). Commodity-like; sourced globally.
- **Component manufacturing.** Outsoles, midsoles, uppers, laces, hardware. Mostly Asia-based, fragmented.
- **Finished-goods manufacturing.** Cut, sew, assemble. Concentrated in Vietnam (50%+ of global athletic footwear), Indonesia (~20%), China (declining, ~15%), India/Cambodia/Bangladesh (rising). Major contract manufacturers: Pou Chen (Taiwan), Feng Tay (Taiwan), Yue Yuen (HK), Shenzhou International (apparel). Brand-owner concentration is high — top 3 manufacturers make ~60% of Nike's footwear.
- **Brand owner.** Design, athlete endorsement, marketing, R&D, distribution strategy, inventory management. The economic profit pool.
- **Wholesale aggregator.** Foot Locker, Dick's Sporting Goods, JD Sports, Sports Direct, run-specialty independents, department stores. Allocates shelf space and decides what consumers see.
- **DTC channel.** Brand owner's own retail stores and .com/app. Captures retailer margin but adds operating cost and inventory risk.
- **Consumer.** Discretionary purchase, fashion-cycle sensitive, brand-loyal at the high end of franchises (Jordan, Air Max), price-sensitive at the bottom.

## Who captures the economics

| Stage | Revenue share | Profit share | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Raw materials & components | ~10% of final retail | ~5% of value-chain profit | Commodity inputs; pricing power minimal except for proprietary tech (Boost) |
| Finished-goods manufacturing | ~10% of final retail | ~10% of value-chain profit | Concentrated suppliers but interchangeable; 4-7% operating margin |
| Brand owner | ~50% of final retail (wholesale price) | ~70% of value-chain profit | Cultural permission, athlete contracts, brand IP. The structural profit pool. |
| Wholesale aggregator | balance of final retail | ~10-15% of value-chain profit | 4-8% operating margin; some pricing power on private label, otherwise margin pressure from brand consolidation |
| DTC retail (brand owner's own) | embedded in brand-owner revenue | additive when full-price; dilutive when promotional | Captures retailer markup but adds fixed cost |

## Moats by stage

- **Brand owner:** brand equity (decades-built), athlete and team contracts (multi-year exclusivity), innovation IP (Vaporfly carbon plate, Boost, CloudTec), retail relationships, manufacturing capacity allocation (top brands get first-pick of factory capacity at peak season).
- **Wholesale aggregator:** physical footprint, real estate, customer data (less than DTC has), category management expertise. Generally a weak moat — Foot Locker's 2023-2025 struggles illustrate.
- **Manufacturer:** scale and process knowledge (Pou Chen / Feng Tay can ramp new SKUs faster than challengers). Real but limited — the brand owner can switch given 12-18 months.
- **Insurgent brands' moat (when achieved):** product credibility on the foot of pros (super-shoe era for Hoka/On), differentiated technology, brand authenticity narrative.

## Disintermediation risk

- **Brand owner DTC bypass of wholesale.** Was the strategic narrative of 2017-2024. Reversed sharply 2024-2026.
- **Direct-to-consumer challenger brands.** On started DTC-led; Allbirds tried (failed). Real risk but historically the DTC-only brands cap out in scale.
- **Resale platforms (StockX, GOAT).** Take a sliver of secondary-market value. Not a structural threat to brand owners' primary economics, but they affect hype-cycle dynamics — the resale market is where brand-heat is most visible.
- **Manufacturer forward integration.** Rare. Pou Chen briefly attempted; not material.
- **Chinese local brands (Anta, Li-Ning).** Effectively disintermediating foreign brands in their home market — taken shelf space, athlete deals, and consumer mindshare from Nike/Adidas in China.

## Vertical integration trends

- **Forward integration (brand owner → retail) was the dominant 2010s trend; pendulum has swung back.** Pure DTC was over-rotated; selective DTC (premium stores, .com, flagship experiences) is the new equilibrium.
- **Backward integration (brand owner → manufacturing) has not happened and likely will not.** The capital intensity, labour management, and geographic complexity argue against it. Brand owners maintain power through specification and design, not ownership.
- **Wholesaler consolidation matters more than vertical integration.** Dick's acquiring Foot Locker (Sept 2025) consolidated ~10% of Nike's revenue into one partner — net positive for Nike strategically (fewer relationships to manage, more pricing discipline) but increases customer concentration risk.
- **Insurgent vertical integration is asymmetric.** On Holding owns more of its retail footprint than Nike (proportionally). Lululemon is overwhelmingly DTC by design. The model works at smaller scale; doesn't scale to Nike size.
