# Creative & design software — primer

**Created:** 2026-05-20
**Last updated:** 2026-05-20
**Framework reference:** `../../frameworks/creative-and-design-software.md`

## TL;DR

This is a category-leader software sector mid-de-rate. Adobe — the dominant incumbent — trades at forward P/E ~10-11x, the cheapest in 15+ years, while still growing revenue 11-12% and generating $9-10B of FCF. The de-rate is driven by an AI-disruption narrative: that generative AI tools (Canva, Figma+AI, Midjourney, Runway, Sora) will commoditize the bottom and disrupt the top of creative software simultaneously, eroding both Adobe's pricing power and its next-generation installed base. The contrarian view — Adobe's response (Firefly built on IP-safe training data, enterprise integration, Express for mass-market, AI ARR more than tripling YoY) is competent and the AI threat is over-discounted at current multiples — is the explicit thesis driving the most interesting risk-reward in mega-cap software right now. Autodesk (CAD/BIM, similar dynamics, slower disruption clock) and Figma (the only AI-era-native challenger that's both public and material) are the cleanest comps. The sector-wide question for the next 3-5 years: **does generative AI value accrue to the workflow owners or to the AI infrastructure layer?** If the former, Adobe and Autodesk are mis-priced; if the latter, multiples may stay compressed for years.

## Industry structure

See `../../frameworks/creative-and-design-software.md` for the general industry treatment. Sector-specific points to flag now:

- The post-Figma-deal era has changed M&A expectations. Transformative horizontal M&A is now widely understood to be regulatorily blocked, which has two consequences: (a) incumbents must build, not buy, in adjacent categories; (b) some private companies that would have been acquirers' targets (Figma, Canva) are now durable independents.
- Adobe's 2024-2026 multiple compression is the most extreme re-rate of any mega-cap software franchise in the last decade. The setup is unusual: revenue growth still mid-double-digit, margins still 35%+, FCF still scaling — but the multiple has compressed from 35-45x forward P/E to 10-11x. This is either a structural break or a value opportunity. The thesis on which side dominates the rest.
- The Adobe Firefly architecture (trained on IP-safe data; commercially indemnified) has emerged as a credible enterprise differentiator vs general-purpose AI (OpenAI, Stability, Midjourney) for customers (Fortune 500 marketers, agencies, ad teams) that can't risk training-data IP exposure. This is the under-appreciated piece of the bull case.

## What's changed in the last 5 years

- **AI architectures became a credible alternative to creative software.** 2022-2023 was the inflection — Midjourney, ChatGPT, Stable Diffusion released; Runway and Pika appeared for video; ElevenLabs and Suno for audio. By 2026, AI-native tools are no longer a niche.
- **Adobe-Figma deal collapsed (Dec 2023, $1B termination fee).** Validated regulatory pushback against horizontal consolidation in software and forced Adobe back to build mode. Figma is now an independent public company (IPO'd late 2024/2025).
- **Adobe shipped Firefly with IP-safe training (2023) and AI credit-based monetization (2024-2026).** Built rather than bought. Q1 FY26 AI ARR tripled YoY, suggesting monetization is starting to compound.
- **Canva moved up-market aggressively** with Canva Enterprise, AI-driven Magic Studio, and consumer-grade pricing for SMB use cases that historically belonged to InDesign/Illustrator.
- **Figma+AI** (Figma AI, Make, FigJam improvements) has narrowed the product-design gap with Adobe XD (Adobe shuttered XD in 2023) — Figma's web-native, multiplayer-by-default architecture is now the industry default in product design and starting to push into adjacent workflows.
- **The pricing power test arrived for Adobe.** Creative Cloud price increases in 2023 and 2024 went through with modest churn impact; the Firefly credit model is testing whether usage-based monetization can sit on top of subscription without alienating customers. So far it's working.
- **SBC dilution scrutiny intensified.** Software investors are now sharply more skeptical of "buyback" claims that are net of heavy SBC issuance. Adobe and Autodesk both screen well on net buyback vs many software peers.

## What's likely to change in the next 5 years

Where consensus might be wrong:

- **AI value accrues primarily to workflow owners, not pure-AI infrastructure.** The contrarian thesis: distributing AI features inside the workflow where designers/marketers/editors already are (Photoshop, Premiere, Acrobat) captures more value than standalone AI tools. Sora and Runway will struggle to monetize at Adobe-like scale because the workflow is the integration point. If true, current Adobe valuation is a steal.
- **Adobe and Figma both grow into their TAMs without one displacing the other.** Most-likely outcome: product design (Figma) and creative production (Adobe) end up being adjacent categories where both grow, rather than one consuming the other. This was the structural reality the Adobe-Figma deal was meant to consolidate around; the market may underestimate that both companies can succeed independently.
- **Canva does NOT scale into the high-end as smoothly as bulls expect.** Enterprise creative is a different sales motion than freemium-to-pro. Canva's path to $5B+ revenue exists but is harder than the consensus 2023-2024 view assumed.
- **Tariff-style policy threats to global creative software grow.** EU and India regulation on AI training data, US export controls on AI features, China's continued exclusion of US software — all chip at the global TAM at the margin.
- **Microsoft Designer becomes a real threat to Adobe Express but NOT to Photoshop/Illustrator.** Designer is bundled into M365 and reaches hundreds of millions of users; it competes for the consumer-design segment where Adobe Express plays. The pro creative workflows remain Adobe-centric.

## Where economic profit pools sit

The economic profit in creative software flows overwhelmingly to **the workflow owner** — the company whose software has the file-format lock-in and the trained installed base. Adobe captures the largest single share in commercial creative; Autodesk in CAD/BIM; Figma in product design; Canva in mass-market. The combined operating income of the top-5 creative software companies is roughly $15-18B/year on roughly $35-40B of revenue — operating margins of 40-50% are normal.

Within Adobe specifically, the profit mix tilts toward Digital Media (Creative Cloud + Document Cloud, ~75% of revenue) at higher GAAP margins than Digital Experience (~25% of revenue, lower margins, more services-heavy). Inside Digital Media, the Document Cloud / Acrobat franchise is unusually profitable — PDF is the most successful workflow lock-in in software history, and Acrobat extracts rent from it across consumer, SMB, and enterprise channels with minimal product-investment intensity.

**AI infrastructure providers (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google AI) capture infrastructure revenue but not workflow profit.** Token-priced API revenue is structurally lower margin than seat-based subscription. The question of where the profit pool migrates over the next 5 years is unsettled but the structural argument favours workflow owners.

## Open questions

- **Does AI value accrue to workflow owners (Adobe, Autodesk, Figma) or infrastructure providers (OpenAI, Anthropic)?** The single highest-conviction-changing question. Currently unsettled. Watch monetization data — Adobe AI ARR vs OpenAI enterprise revenue trajectories side-by-side.
- **Is Adobe's "new creator" cohort losing share to AI-native tools, or just delaying entry?** This is the structural question Adobe's reported numbers can't fully answer. Watch Adobe Express subscriber data and demographic breakdowns where available.
- **Can Figma sustainably grow into adjacent categories (whiteboarding, slides, design files-as-source-of-truth for product) at Adobe-like margins?** If yes, Figma is the structural insurgent; if not, both companies coexist comfortably.
- **What's the realistic 5-year operating margin for Adobe given AI compute costs?** AI inference is not free. Whether Adobe maintains 35%+ non-GAAP operating margin while serving 100M+ users with AI features is an open empirical question.
- **Does Microsoft Designer / Loop / Copilot Designer eventually become the productivity-tier creative tool for the broad enterprise?** If yes, Adobe Express has a tougher path; the pro franchises remain unaffected.
