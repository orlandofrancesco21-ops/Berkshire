## What the business does

UnitedHealth Group is the largest US managed care + health-services company, organized into two segments. **UnitedHealthcare (~55% of revenue, ~$310B in 2025)** is the insurance arm — commercial (~26M members), Medicare Advantage (~7.5M members after Q1 2026 attrition of 965K), Medicaid (~7.5M members), and other government programs. **Optum (~45% of revenue, ~$257B in 2026 guidance)** is the integrated services arm split into three sub-segments: **OptumRx (PBM)** processes ~1.6B annual scripts and is the third-largest US PBM; **Optum Health** operates ~2,000 affiliated clinics, surgery centers, and home health services across 31 markets, serving ~100M consumer touchpoints; **Optum Insight** provides analytics, software, and revenue cycle management — including the Change Healthcare claims-clearinghouse infrastructure (acquired 2022, breached February 2024). Total 2025 revenue was ~$400B+ at non-GAAP operating margin of ~6.5%, generating ~$25B operating cash flow. The company is led by Stephen Hemsley, who returned as CEO in May 2025 after Andrew Witty stepped down — Hemsley previously ran UNH from 2006-2017 and built the modern Optum franchise. The vertical-integration thesis — that combining payer + PBM + clinics generates structural margin advantages and steers consumer healthcare into a UNH ecosystem — has been the core 2017-2023 growth story and is now under simultaneous attack from medical cost trend, restrictive MA rates, DOJ investigations, the Change Healthcare cyberattack aftermath, and the political backlash that followed the December 2024 assassination of UnitedHealthcare CEO Brian Thompson.

## The bull case

Three framework-grounded points:

1. **The 2024-2026 reset is cyclical and the franchise survives intact.** Managed care is a sector that has reset roughly every 8-10 years (1992, 2004, 2014-2015, now 2024-2026); each prior reset was followed by 3-5 years of 2-3x stock returns at the survivors with intact franchises. The 2014-2015 UnitedHealth itself reset on MA cost trend acceleration, then compounded ~4x over the following 5 years. The current reset has the recognizable cyclical structure: cost trend acceleration, restrictive MA rate cycle, MLR blowout, earnings reset, multiple compression. MLR is already improving (Q1 2026: 83.9%, +90 bps YoY). The 2026 EPS guidance of $17.75+ is the bottom; consensus 2027 is ~$22 and 2028 is $25-27 — recovering toward the prior $26+ run-rate. At $393 vs $25-28 normalized FY28 EPS, the stock is at 14-16x forward two-year P/E — bottom-quartile for UNH historically, with a recovery slope already partially visible. The framework's #1 great-business trait (scale advantage in claims processing and provider network leverage) is unchallenged; UNH's scale moat is intact.

2. **Capital allocation is exceptional and Hemsley back is the right operator.** UnitedHealth has compounded dividends at double-digit rates for 15 consecutive years (current annualized $8.40/share = ~2.1% yield, top of 15-year range), bought back ~$9B in stock in 2024, and the FCF generation continues even through the reset. Hemsley's return is structurally significant — he built modern UnitedHealth (2006-2017) and ran the most successful capital-allocation playbook in the sector's history during that period. The framework's "counter-cyclical buybacks at MLR-blowout troughs is the single best capital allocation decision in the industry" applies directly to the 2025-2026 window; UNH's history is heavy with examples of Hemsley-era buybacks at the right multiples (2014-2015 specifically), and there's reason to expect this management to repeat the pattern. **The combination of (a) intact franchise + (b) trough-cycle earnings + (c) returning architect-CEO + (d) historically disciplined capital allocator is the same setup that produced UNH's 2015-2020 compounding decade.**

3. **The Optum integration thesis is bruised but not broken.** Optum Rx (PBM) continues to grow profitably with intact rebate economics; Optum Health is restructuring toward its 6-8% long-term margin target and Q1 2026 showed early progress; Optum Insight is recovering from the Change Healthcare cyber-cost cycle. The framework warning that "services revenue growth without operating income growth is rent-extraction, not value creation" applies most to Optum Health 2022-2024 (which underperformed) — but Hemsley's stated restructuring direction is to right-size that subsegment, which is exactly the right response. The PBM business, the highest-margin component of Optum, is still intact regulatory pressure notwithstanding — and the FTC investigation has resulted in noise rather than structural changes through 2026. Once the regulatory clouds resolve, the integrated payer-services franchise retains genuine scale and data advantages that pure-play peers (ELV, HUM, CNC) cannot replicate.

## The bear case

Three framework-grounded points. Steelmanned:

1. **The DOJ investigations are an existential overhang and Berkshire's exit at near-current prices is the relevant pricing signal.** UnitedHealth faces concurrent DOJ criminal AND civil investigations specifically targeting Medicare Advantage upcoding practices. The 2024-2026 political environment is hostile to MA risk-coding intensity in ways that did not exist in any prior reset cycle. Possible outcomes include: civil settlement with operational covenants (likely); financial penalties in the billions (probable); structural changes to MA risk-adjustment economics that compress the most profitable segment's margins permanently (~30% probability); criminal indictments of executives (low but non-zero probability). The framework's "regulatory ceiling on MA upcoding revenue" warning is exactly what the DOJ is enforcing. **Most diagnostically: Berkshire Hathaway built a UNH position in Q2 2025 (revealed August 2025) and exited entirely by Q1 2026 (revealed in 13F filed May 15, 2026)** — a roughly 9-month round-trip that's highly atypical for Berkshire's slow portfolio rhythm. The likeliest interpretations: (a) the rally from $270 to $400 closed the margin of safety Berkshire identified, suggesting current pricing has run past their fair-value zone; (b) deeper diligence on the DOJ exposure revealed structural risk Berkshire wasn't willing to underwrite at any price. Either reading argues against initiating size at $393.

2. **The reset may be structural, not cyclical, and consensus 2028 EPS is too optimistic.** The 2024-2026 setup differs from prior resets in ways the cyclical thesis under-weights: (a) GLP-1 cost trend is a structural long-tail driver, not a transient pulse; (b) MA rate compression reflects CMS policy direction, not just a one-cycle tightening — 2027-2028 rates may be modestly better but not constructive enough to restore prior MA economics; (c) STAR rating tightening is multi-year and durable; (d) prior-auth political momentum will translate into operational constraints regardless of who controls Congress; (e) the Brian Thompson assassination crystallized a consumer/political hostility toward UnitedHealthcare specifically that affects member preference and political pressure on its specific brand. **If steady-state operating margin is closer to 5% than 6.5% and MA grows 5% not 10%, normalized EPS is closer to $20-22 than $25-27.** At $393 / $21 = 19x P/E — not bottom-quartile, just average for the sector. The framework's "trough multiple on trough earnings" trap is the active risk: the stock at $393 looks reasonable on consensus 2028, but consensus may be modelling a faster and fuller recovery than reality supports.

3. **The 47% rally from the March low has already captured the easy compression-to-normal trade.** UNH bottomed at ~$270 in March 2026 — that was the genuinely-mispriced moment when MLR data, MA membership losses, and DOJ headlines all peaked simultaneously. From $270 at, say, 10-12x forward 2028 EPS, the asymmetry was extreme. At $393 (current), the same forward EPS implies 14-16x — still cheap, but not a generational mispricing anymore. **The risk-reward has materially compressed:** the upside from here to a successful turnaround at 18x forward two-year EPS = ~$470 (+20%); the downside on a DOJ-driven structural rerating to 12x normalized depressed EPS = ~$260 (-34%). That asymmetry (~0.6:1 reward-to-risk) is worse than acceptable for a meaningful position. **The wise play was buying in March; the current price asks for trust on regulatory resolution that hasn't happened and on operating execution that's months from being demonstrable.**

## Capital allocation track record

Five-year capital allocation data (FY21-FY25, calendar years):

| Year | FCF (~$B) | Buybacks ($B) | Dividends ($B) | M&A | Capex (~$B) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| FY21 | ~22 | 4.4 | 5.2 | Change Healthcare announced ($13B, closed 2022) | 2.7 |
| FY22 | ~20 | 7.0 | 6.1 | Change Healthcare closed; multiple Optum bolt-ons | 2.7 |
| FY23 | ~29 | 8.5 | 6.8 | Bolt-ons only | 3.4 |
| FY24 | ~24 | 9.0 | 7.5 | Bolt-ons | 3.5 |
| FY25 | ~18-20 (depressed) | ~6-7 | ~7.8 | Reduced M&A; restructuring Optum Health | 3.0 |

**Read:** mixed historically; very good before 2022, less good 2022-2024, recovery in 2025-2026 TBD. The Change Healthcare acquisition ($13B in 2022) is now demonstrably value-destructive: $4B+ in cyber-remediation costs, antitrust scrutiny, ongoing legal liability. It's the single biggest capital-allocation mistake of the Witty era. **Counter-balancing this**: 15 consecutive years of double-digit dividend increases (one of the longest streaks in the S&P 500), steady buyback execution, no other major M&A missteps, and the dividend has continued through the reset. The Hemsley-era playbook (2006-2017) was textbook framework-compliant — buybacks at low multiples, no transformative M&A, dividend growth funded by FCF. **The forward question**: does Hemsley repeat the playbook at $300-400 stock and ~$25 normalized EPS? If yes, this is the asset best-positioned to compound over 5 years. The Q1 2026 buyback was reduced from the 2024 pace — defensible given regulatory uncertainty, but the framework would prefer aggressive buybacks at current levels if conviction is high.

## Management quality

- **Stephen Hemsley (CEO, May 2025–present).** Returning veteran. Joined UnitedHealth in 1997, CEO 2006-2017, Executive Chairman until 2017. Architect of the Optum buildout and the 2014-2018 dividend/buyback compounding decade. Age 73 — succession-on-arrival is a real watch item. Transcripts since May 2025 read as focused, operational, and explicit about restructuring (Optum Health right-sizing, MA discipline). Framing is "reimagining how we organize, operate, and work" — a multi-year transformation pitch, not a quick fix. **Best-case operator for this specific situation**; the question is how durable his health and tenure are.
- **Andrew Witty (CEO, 2021–May 2025).** Former GSK CEO; tenure ended after the Change Healthcare cyberattack, Thompson assassination, and DOJ investigations all compounded under his watch. Departure described as voluntary but widely read as board-driven.
- **John Rex (CFO, 2014–present).** Long-tenured, steady on disclosure, conservative on guidance. Has navigated the 2024-2026 reset without surprises beyond the macro trend.
- **Tim Noel (CEO of UnitedHealthcare, post-Thompson assassination).** Promoted to lead the insurance segment after Brian Thompson's December 2024 murder. Less public track record at the segment-CEO level; situation is in stabilization mode.
- **Heather Cianfrocco (CEO of Optum, January 2025–present).** Promoted to lead Optum after restructuring. Long-tenured at Optum (joined 2011, ran OptumRx). Solid operator.
- **Reading transcripts (Q1 2025 through Q1 2026):** disclosure quality has been good — UNH has not hidden the Optum Health margin pressure, MA membership losses, or MLR pressure. Hemsley specifically has been direct about scale of operational restructuring required ("not just being more productive, but reimagining"). The board appears engaged; reduced dividend growth and slowed buybacks are consistent with cash preservation against regulatory uncertainty — defensible if not framework-optimal.

**Read:** management quality is materially better post-Hemsley than during the late-Witty period. The single biggest risk is Hemsley's age and succession (he's 73, was last CEO until 57). A successor has not been clearly identified externally.

## What would change my mind

**Bull-case kill switches (signals that would invalidate the bull thesis):**

- DOJ produces a structural settlement (forced PBM divestiture, MA risk-adjustment clawback) rather than civil monetary penalties.
- MLR fails to continue improving sequentially in Q2-Q4 2026; MCR remains above 86%.
- MA membership losses continue at 500K+/quarter through 2026.
- 2027 CMS MA rate notice is restrictive (worse than 2026 base case).
- Hemsley steps back or is medically unable to continue before a credible successor is in place.
- Optum Health continues to underperform; restructuring fails to drive EBIT recovery.
- Another major regulatory/legal event (new DOJ action, FTC enforcement on PBM, state-level action).
- Material dividend cut (would be the cleanest signal that management has lost confidence in cash generation).

**Bear-case kill switches (signals that the bear case is wrong):**

- DOJ resolution emerges with civil settlement + operational covenants but no structural change to MA economics.
- MLR returns to 84% or below by Q4 2026.
- MA membership stabilizes or returns to growth at 4-star+ plans by H2 2026.
- Optum Health margin recovers to 6%+ on a four-quarter trailing basis.
- 2027 MA rate notice is constructive (favourable spread vs cost trend).
- Hemsley executes aggressive buyback at sub-$400 stock during 2026-2027.
- Earnings guidance for 2027 emerges above $22+ adjusted EPS.

## Conviction

**Level (1-5):** 2 (modest)

**Why this level:** The asymmetry has compressed materially after the 47% rally from March. At $393:
- **Upside:** to ~$470-500 (+20-27%) on successful turnaround, vs to ~$600 (+53%) on aggressive bull case requiring multiple recovery + full margin restoration.
- **Downside:** to ~$280-310 (-21-29%) on DOJ structural ruling or extended MLR pressure, vs ~$200 (-49%) on combined structural reset + dividend cut scenario.

Reward-to-risk is roughly 1:1 at base case, which is below the threshold for meaningful sizing. The franchise quality is real, but **the price is no longer attractive enough to overcome the regulatory and operational tail risks**. Berkshire's exit at near-current prices is the relevant external signal — the smartest known long-only investor took the round trip from initiation to exit between Q3 2025 and Q1 2026 inclusive, ending in a sale that happened around current levels.

The conviction would step up materially on either (a) a price retreat toward $300 (returning the asymmetry to attractive); or (b) DOJ resolution clarity, which would compress the downside scenarios.
