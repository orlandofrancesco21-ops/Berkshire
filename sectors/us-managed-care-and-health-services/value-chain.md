# US managed care & health services — value chain

## Value chain map

Premium-payer (employer, CMS, state, individual) → Insurer underwriting → Network of providers (hospitals, physicians, ancillaries) → Patient (the insured member) → Claims processor → PBM (drug benefit) → Pharmaceutical manufacturers.

The integrated payers (UNH, CVS, Cigna) own multiple links: insurance underwriting + PBM + (variously) clinics, surgery centers, home health, analytics.

- **Premium-payer.** Employer (commercial), CMS (Medicare Advantage), state Medicaid agencies, individual exchange enrollees. The economic customer.
- **Insurer underwriting.** Accepts risk for a fixed monthly fee per member. Sets benefit design, networks, premium pricing.
- **Provider network.** Hospitals (HCA, THC, UHS, non-profits), physician groups (independent, employed, payer-owned via Optum/CenterWell), ancillary (imaging, labs, surgery centers). Negotiates rates with insurers.
- **Claims processing.** Adjudication, payment, denial. Historically the back office; increasingly a profit center via tech-enabled analytics (Optum Insight, Cotiviti, Change Healthcare).
- **PBM.** Drug benefit management. Three large PBMs (Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx) control ~75% of US prescription volume. Revenue model: spread between drug rebates retained and rebates passed through; admin fees from plan sponsors; specialty pharmacy markups.
- **Patient.** The member. Has minimal direct relationship with insurer except at point of denial; co-pays and deductibles are the consumer-facing economics.
- **Pharmaceutical manufacturers.** Pay rebates to PBMs to secure formulary placement. The pharma-PBM rebate structure is the most contested economic arrangement in US healthcare and the target of FTC and Congressional investigation.

## Who captures the economics

| Stage | Revenue share | Profit share | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Insurance underwriting | ~50% of total | ~30-40% | High revenue, thin margins (4-6%) |
| PBM | ~25% (pharma manufacturer rebates flow through) | ~20-25% | Higher margin (5-7%) but politically exposed |
| Provider services (clinics, surgery, home health) | ~10-15% | ~15-20% | Higher margin (7-12%) when integrated well |
| Analytics/claims tech | ~5% | ~10% | Highest margin (~20%+) but volatile post-Change Healthcare |
| Pharmaceutical manufacturers (upstream) | n/a — separate value chain | n/a | The largest profit pool in US healthcare overall but separate from managed care |

## Moats by stage

- **Insurance underwriting:** scale (claims processing cost per claim), network leverage (deeper provider discounts at scale), regulatory compliance (only the largest can afford compliance infrastructure), broker distribution relationships. Real but commoditizing.
- **PBM:** scale of script volume (rebate negotiation power), formulary control as bargaining lever with pharma, specialty pharmacy distribution. Strong moat but increasingly regulated.
- **Clinic/services rollups:** geographic density, payer-provider integration, employed-physician retention. Variable moat depending on execution.
- **Analytics:** data assets (claims data is the single most valuable healthcare dataset), customer integration. Moat compromised at UNH/Optum Insight post-Change Healthcare cyberattack.

## Disintermediation risk

- **Direct-to-employer health benefit alternatives.** ICHRAs, level-funded plans, narrow-network direct contracts — chipping at traditional fully-insured commercial. Real but slow.
- **Government takeover scenarios.** Medicare-for-All, public option, expanded Medicaid eligibility. Tail risk; probability low in current political environment but not zero.
- **PBM bypass.** Pharma-direct-to-employer pricing (e.g. Lilly direct), Mark Cuban's Cost Plus Drugs. Eroding rebate-spread economics at the margin.
- **AI-native prior-auth and care navigation.** Could shift bargaining power between payer and provider but doesn't disintermediate insurance.
- **Centralized provider consolidation.** Hospital systems gaining bargaining leverage erodes payer margins in specific geographies; mitigated by payer's national scale and member-direction tools.

## Vertical integration trends

- **Payer → PBM integration** is the most successful and durable vertical play. UNH-Catamaran (2015), CVS-Caremark (2007), Cigna-Express Scripts (2018) all created durable cross-segment economics.
- **Payer → clinic integration** has been more mixed. Optum Health (UNH) is the largest experiment — credible at scale but underperforming margin targets. CVS-Oak Street is still being digested. Humana CenterWell home health is the cleanest success story; Humana also has primary care via Conviva and CenterWell Primary Care.
- **Payer → analytics integration** has been mixed-to-poor. Optum Insight inherited Change Healthcare's regulatory exposure and the 2024 cyberattack. The "data is the moat" thesis from 2017-2022 has been complicated by data-security and antitrust scrutiny.
- **Provider → payer integration** (e.g. Kaiser, Geisinger, Intermountain) works regionally but doesn't scale. Provider-led ACOs in Medicare are the lighter version of this.
- **Pharma → PBM integration** has been blocked repeatedly by FTC; no major precedent.
- **The current regulatory direction** is to question rather than encourage integration. FTC investigation of PBMs is the most active example; antitrust skepticism of payer M&A continues.
