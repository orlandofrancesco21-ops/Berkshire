## TL;DR

The sector is mid-reset on the most violent earnings-and-multiple compression since 2008 — driven by (a) medical cost trend acceleration that pricing cycles couldn't catch (GLP-1 utilization, behavioural health, outpatient inflation); (b) restrictive 2024-2026 Medicare Advantage rate cycles compounded by tighter STAR rating thresholds and risk-adjustment intensity caps; (c) concurrent DOJ criminal and civil investigations into MA upcoding at UNH, Humana, and Elevance; (d) the December 2024 assassination of Brian Thompson (CEO of UnitedHealthcare) which crystallized political and consumer hostility into a regulatory accelerant. UNH specifically reset 2026 adjusted EPS guidance to $17.75 (down from prior 2025 levels of $26+), brought back Stephen Hemsley as CEO (the architect of UNH's 2006-2017 buildout), and has rallied 47% from a March 2026 low of $270 to ~$393 currently. **Berkshire Hathaway built then completely exited a UNH position inside a 9-month window** — the kind of round trip Berkshire rarely does, and a notable signal at current pricing. The contrarian view worth examining: managed care is a cyclical industry whose multi-decade pattern is reset every ~8-10 years (1992, 2004, 2014-2015, now 2024-2026), and the survivors with intact franchises (UNH, ELV) double or triple from trough multiples within 3-5 years if the franchise survives the reset. The consensus view: this reset is different because the regulatory pressure on MA and PBM economics is structural, not cyclical. Both sides are partly right; the question for any investment is which side the current price embeds and whether margin of safety still exists after a 47% recovery.

## Industry structure

See `../../frameworks/us-managed-care-and-health-services.md`. Sector-specific points to flag now:

- The MA rate cycle is the single biggest sector-level catalyst over the next 24 months. CMS announces 2027 rates in early 2026 (already done — modest improvement) and 2028 rates in early 2027. A constructive 2027-2028 rate cycle is the necessary condition for sector-wide recovery; restrictive rates would extend the compression.
- Cigna spun off / divested most of its MA exposure to HCSC in 2024 — meaningful in that Cigna is now cleaner as a "commercial + PBM" pure play, while the remaining MA-heavy names (HUM, UNH, ELV, ALHC, CNC's MA exposure) bear the rate-cycle risk concentrated.
- The Brian Thompson assassination (December 2024) is genuinely a sector-level event. Beyond the tragedy itself, it accelerated bipartisan political momentum for prior-auth reform, MA upcoding restrictions, and PBM transparency — all of which were on the agenda before but now have political fuel.
- The Change Healthcare cyberattack (February 2024, UNH-owned) cost $4B+ in remediation, exposed 100M+ patient records, and remains operationally relevant to UNH financial reporting through 2025-2026.

## What's changed in the last 5 years

The shifts that matter for forward-looking thesis work:

- **Medical cost trend accelerated structurally.** GLP-1 utilization growth (Wegovy, Zepbound) added 100-150 bps to commercial cost trend at the margin; behavioural health utilization remained elevated post-pandemic; outpatient pricing inflation outpaced overall CPI. Premium pricing was set 12-18 months in advance and missed.
- **Medicare Advantage rate cycle turned restrictive.** 2023, 2024, and 2025 MA rate updates were below cost trend after adjustment for risk score normalization. CMS also tightened STAR rating cut points (effectively penalizing existing plans) and announced multi-year risk-adjustment intensity restrictions.
- **DOJ accelerated MA upcoding enforcement.** Criminal and civil investigations announced 2025 against UNH; ongoing whistleblower cases against multiple insurers. The legal posture has shifted from technical-compliance disputes to fraud-framed enforcement.
- **The Change Healthcare cyberattack (Feb 2024).** Largest healthcare data breach in US history. Crystallized concentration risk in healthcare claims processing infrastructure.
- **Brian Thompson assassination (Dec 2024).** Cultural and political accelerant for regulatory change.
- **Cigna's MA divestiture to HCSC (2024).** Marked a strategic retreat from MA by a major payer — a notable signal about MA's prospective economics.
- **CEO turnover.** Andrew Witty out at UNH (May 2025), Stephen Hemsley back. Brian Thompson murder removed the UnitedHealthcare CEO. Karen Lynch out at CVS (Oct 2024), David Joyner in. Major C-suite churn across the sector.
- **The Optum integration thesis fractured.** Optum Health (the clinic rollup) underperformed; Optum Insight (Change Healthcare-heavy) absorbed massive cyber costs; only Optum Rx (PBM) held up cleanly. The "Optum is the value driver" story that drove UNH's 2017-2022 multiple expansion is being re-evaluated.

## What's likely to change in the next 5 years

Where consensus might be wrong:

- **2027-2028 MA rate cycle constructive.** Base case: after 3 years of restrictive rates, CMS produces a positive-spread rate cycle in 2027 or 2028. This is the necessary catalyst for sector recovery. Watching CMS notices in Feb 2026 (already done — modest improvement) and Feb 2027.
- **Cost trend normalizes 2026-2027.** GLP-1 utilization plateaus; behavioural health stabilizes; pricing catches up. Base case: industry MLRs return to 84-86% range from current 86-88% range.
- **DOJ resolutions emerge by 2027-2028.** Most likely outcome: civil settlements with operational covenants (mandated risk-coding audits, prior-auth restrictions, monitor appointments) but no structural break-up. The structural-change scenarios (forced PBM divestiture, MA upcoding clawbacks) remain real tail risks at 15-25% probability each.
- **PBM regulation tightens but doesn't dismantle.** State-level rebate transparency rules expand; federal FTC actions force operational changes; net effect is 100-200 bps margin compression in the PBM line, not elimination of the business.
- **Prior-auth automation accelerates.** Both AI-driven prior-auth processing (controversial) and regulatory mandates for faster/automated approvals reshape the operational economics. Net financial effect is unclear; consumer experience effect is positive.
- **Vertical integration continues but at lower multiples.** Operators keep building services but pay less for assets. The 2021-2023 "buy clinic networks at any multiple" strategy is over.

## Where economic profit pools sit

Most of the economic profit in this value chain sits with **integrated PBMs** (Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx) — the rebate-retention and formulary-control economics are the highest-margin layer in the system, generating $5-10B of EBIT each at the top 3 PBMs on a relatively modest revenue base of $100-200B. The **insurance underwriting layer** itself generates roughly $5-7B of EBIT at the top integrated players on revenue of $200-300B — large absolute dollars but thin margins. **Services segments** (Optum Health clinics, Cigna Evernorth specialty pharmacy, Humana CenterWell home health) generate $3-6B of EBIT each at the top players but at variable margins depending on whether the integration thesis is real or rhetorical at that specific subsegment.

Two important under-appreciated points: (a) the **Medicare Advantage profit pool** at the integrated players is disproportionately large relative to its share of revenue — MA generates 30-40% of operating income at UNH and Humana despite being closer to 25% of revenue, because of risk-adjustment economics that the DOJ is now investigating; and (b) the **PBM profit pool is structurally separable from the insurance pool** — if regulation forces divestiture or restructuring of PBMs from payers, the SOTP economics change significantly.

## Open questions

- **Is the 2024-2026 reset cyclical or structural?** The dominant question. If cyclical, the 2014-2015 playbook applies and survivors compound 2-3x from trough. If structural, multiples stay compressed for 5-10 years even as earnings recover modestly.
- **What is the steady-state operating margin for MA after CMS rate normalization and post-DOJ resolution?** Consensus models 6-8%; bear models 4-5%; bull models 8-10%. The difference is worth ~$5-10 on UNH's fair value.
- **Why exactly did Berkshire exit UNH between Q3 2025 and Q1 2026?** The 13F shows the action but not the reason. Possibilities: closed margin of safety; regulatory diligence reveal; portfolio reallocation. Material for anyone using current pricing.
- **Does the Hemsley return work?** Hemsley built modern UNH from 2006-2017 and ran the most successful capital-allocation playbook in the sector's history. At 73 and after 8 years away, can he reset the operating model and survival the regulatory cycle?
- **Will Optum Health be divested, restructured, or rebuilt?** Hemsley has hinted at operational restructuring; the strategic question is whether the clinic rollup thesis is viable at all.
- **Does the political/regulatory hostility crystallize into structural change** (forced PBM divestiture, prior-auth restrictions, MA rate clawbacks) or fade with cycle recovery?
